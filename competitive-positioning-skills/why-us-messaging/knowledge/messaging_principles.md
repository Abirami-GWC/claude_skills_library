# Why-Us Messaging Principles

## 1. Outcomes over features, always

Weak: "We offer real-time threat detection with AI-powered analytics."
Strong: "You know about a breach in minutes, not weeks — the gap most security
teams hit with [Competitor]'s batch-based scanning."

Every feature claim should answer "so what changes for this specific persona,"
not just state a capability.

## 2. Persona changes the story, not the facts

The underlying differentiator is the same; the framing changes by who's reading:

| Persona | Cares about | Framing example |
|---|---|---|
| CEO | Business risk, growth, competitive position | "Faster time-to-value means the initiative shows results before the next board review." |
| CTO / VP Eng | Architecture, scalability, technical debt, integration | "Native API-first design means your team isn't maintaining brittle middleware." |
| CFO / Procurement | Total cost of ownership, predictability, ROI | "Usage-based pricing means you're not paying for idle seats during slow quarters." |
| Product Manager | Speed to ship, workflow fit, extensibility | "Your team ships the integration in days, not a quarter-long project." |
| IT Manager | Reliability, support burden, security operations | "Fewer support tickets because deployment doesn't require a specialized team." |
| End user / practitioner | Day-to-day friction, learning curve | "Nobody needs a week of training to be productive on day one." |

See `knowledge/persona_priorities.json` for the full structured map.

## 3. The "acknowledge, then pivot" pattern

Every strong narrative concedes something true about the competitor before
pivoting. This builds credibility and defuses the buyer's own counter-argument
before they raise it.

Template: "[Competitor] is a solid choice if [genuine strength]. Where teams
tend to hit a wall is [specific limitation, relevant to this persona] — that's
the gap we built [product/feature] to close."

## 4. Three differentiator types

| Type | Best for persona | Example framing |
|---|---|---|
| **Capability gap** | CTO, IT Manager, Product Manager | "X requires a separate module for this; it's native here." |
| **Economic gap** | CFO, Procurement | "X's per-seat pricing scales against you as you grow; ours is usage-based." |
| **Operational gap** | IT Manager, End user, anyone burned by a rival before | "Teams migrating from X most often cite [support/onboarding/reliability]." |

Pick the type(s) that match what the target persona actually optimizes for.

## 5. Length and structure by channel

- **Website copy**: punchy headline + 1–2 supporting sentences per
  differentiator, scannable, no jargon.
- **Talk track (spoken)**: 3–5 short sentences, said out loud, one
  differentiator per breath.
- **Email**: 80–120 words, one clear differentiator, soft call to action.
- **Deck slide notes**: bullet-length claims on the slide, full sentences in
  speaker notes.

## 6. Fairness and credibility guardrails

- Never claim a competitor "can't" do something they do differently or charge
  extra for — say so precisely.
- Avoid absolute language ("always," "never," "the only") unless literally,
  verifiably true.
- A narrative that would embarrass the seller if the competitor's rep read it
  aloud is too aggressive — aim for "confident and fair."

## 7. Proof beats assertion

Strengthen every differentiator claim with a specific number, a named
(permissioned) or anonymized customer proof point, or a third-party reference.
Without one, downgrade the confidence of the phrasing ("customers tell us..."
instead of "the market agrees...").
