# Pricing psychology

Background on why price objections behave differently depending on timing, underlying the
response frames in `handlers/pricing-objection-handler.md` and
`playbooks/renewal-and-upsell-objections.md`.

## Price objections mean different things at different points in a conversation

Price objections early in a conversation are often a value objection in disguise — the prospect
hasn't yet connected cost to a problem worth solving. Price objections *late*, after value has
been established, are more likely to be real budget constraints. Calibrate how much weight to give
a price objection by where in the conversation it lands, not just its wording.

This is why the handler's default move is to reframe to total cost of ownership (incident response
cost, downtime cost, staff time saved) rather than defend the sticker price directly — TCO reframing
only works if it's genuine, but it's the right first move regardless of whether the objection is
really about value or really about budget, since it surfaces which one it is.

## Why never state a number

Beyond the obvious commercial risk, stating a discount number in the moment also short-circuits
the diagnostic value of the pricing conversation — once a number is on the table, the prospect
stops explaining what's actually driving the pushback (budget freeze vs. prioritization vs. a
competing quote) and starts negotiating the number instead. Routing to the approved pricing owner
keeps that diagnostic conversation open a little longer.

## Renewal pricing carries extra weight

At renewal, a price objection has a different psychological backdrop than a new-deal one — there's
a loss-aversion component (an existing tool that already "belongs" to the team feels harder to give
up on principle than to negotiate down), which is part of why
`playbooks/renewal-and-upsell-objections.md` recommends grounding renewal conversations in the
account's own usage data rather than a generic value pitch: it's more credible to an existing
customer than a new abstract case.
