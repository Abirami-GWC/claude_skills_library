# Roleplay transcript example

A full sample session showing the expected format from
`handlers/objection-roleplay-simulator.md`, for reference when running a real session.

---

**Rep (user):** Let's practice pricing objections.

**Claude (in character, as prospect):** *"Honestly, this is a lot more than what we're paying
right now. I'm not sure I can justify it internally."*

**Rep (user):** I hear you — can I ask what you're comparing it against? Sometimes the current
setup has hidden costs that don't show up on the invoice.

**Claude (in character):**
> *"Fair point. We're paying for the licenses and then our IT team handles incident response
> in-house, so I guess that's not really counted anywhere."*
>
> Coach note: good move asking what's being compared — that's exactly the total-cost-of-ownership
> reframe from the pricing handler. Next, push a little further and ask roughly how much time IT
> spends on incident response, so the comparison has a number attached instead of staying abstract.

**Rep (user):** Roughly how much time does your team spend on incident response in a typical
month?

**Claude (in character):**
> *"I'd have to check, but it's probably more than we'd like to admit."*
>
> Coach note: that's a soft opening, not a hard stall — the prospect is signaling there IS a
> number, they just don't have it memorized. Good spot to offer to help them find it (e.g. "want
> me to send a quick worksheet your team can fill in?") rather than dropping the thread here.

---

**End-of-session summary (given on request):**

What landed well: opened with a question instead of a rebuttal, stayed away from stating a
specific discount, and kept the conversation moving toward a concrete comparison.

What to tighten: when the prospect gave a soft, non-numeric answer ("more than we'd like to
admit"), the rep could have offered a concrete next step (a worksheet, a quick audit call) instead
of letting the thread trail off — that's the difference between an interesting exchange and one
that produces a next action.
