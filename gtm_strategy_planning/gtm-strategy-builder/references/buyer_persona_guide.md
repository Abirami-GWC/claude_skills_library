# Buyer Persona Guide

**Purpose of this document:** Give Claude a standard, evidence-oriented method for constructing buyer personas that are useful for messaging and sales enablement, independent of industry.

## Introduction

A buyer persona is a research-grounded representation of an individual role involved in the buying decision — distinct from the ICP, which describes the target account/organization (see `icp_framework.md`). Personas exist to make messaging, content, and sales conversations specific to real human motivations, rather than written generically for "the customer." Most B2B and complex B2C purchases involve more than one persona (economic buyer, technical/user buyer, influencer, blocker), and a GTM strategy should account for each relevant one.

## Detailed Explanation

### Persona Roles in a Buying Committee
For considered purchases, it is useful to distinguish:
- **Economic buyer** — controls or approves budget; cares about ROI, risk, and strategic fit.
- **User/champion buyer** — will use the product day-to-day; cares about usability, workflow fit, and personal effectiveness.
- **Technical evaluator** — assesses feasibility, security, integration, compliance; cares about risk mitigation and technical criteria.
- **Influencer** — shapes opinion without formal authority (e.g., a respected peer, an internal advisor).
- **Blocker/risk owner** — a role (often legal, security, procurement, or a skeptical stakeholder) whose objections must be resolved even though they are not the primary buyer.

Simple/self-serve purchases may collapse several of these into one individual; complex enterprise or capital purchases typically separate them across multiple people, and messaging must address each distinctly.

### Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD) Foundation
The most durable personas are built around the **job the persona is "hiring" the product to do**, not demographic traits. A JTBD-style persona captures:
- **Functional job:** the practical task or outcome the person needs accomplished.
- **Emotional job:** how they want to feel (or avoid feeling) in the process (e.g., confident, not blamed, in control).
- **Social job:** how they want to be perceived by others (e.g., as innovative, as a careful steward of budget).
- **Triggering situation:** the circumstance that causes them to actively seek a solution now (a JTBD-style "struggling moment").

Demographic and firmographic detail (title, industry, company size) should be included only insofar as it affects the job and context — not as filler detail with no bearing on messaging.

### Persona Components (Full Template Fields)
A complete, usable persona includes: role/title variations, where this role sits in the buying committee, primary goals, primary pains/frustrations with the status quo, functional/emotional/social jobs, objections/concerns typically raised, information sources trusted (channels/content types), decision criteria this role weighs most heavily, and a short "how we help" statement tailored to this role specifically (not a copy of the general value proposition).

### Personas Are Not Fictional Biographies
A common failure mode is spending effort on cosmetic biographical detail (a stock photo, an invented name, hobbies) that has no bearing on messaging or channel decisions. Persona work should prioritize the fields that change what is said or how it is delivered: jobs, pains, objections, decision criteria, and trusted channels. Cosmetic detail is optional flavor, not the substance of the persona.

## Professional Guidance

Build personas from real signal when available: sales call notes, support tickets, win/loss interviews, and customer success feedback. When no such data exists, construct a hypothesis-driven persona explicitly labeled as such, and recommend the specific validation step (e.g., "validate with 5 customer interviews focused on the technical evaluator's actual security checklist"). Always map each persona back to the ICP(s) it appears within, and ensure messaging pillars for that persona directly answer that persona's stated objections and decision criteria (see `messaging_framework.md`).

## Examples

- *B2B SaaS, technical evaluator persona:* A DevOps lead evaluating a monitoring tool; functional job = reduce mean-time-to-detect incidents; emotional job = avoid being blamed for outages; decision criteria = integration with existing alerting stack, security posture, on-call disruption risk.
- *Healthcare, economic buyer persona:* A hospital compliance director evaluating documentation software; functional job = pass the next accreditation audit without a scramble; emotional job = confidence, not last-minute panic; decision criteria = audit-trail completeness, staff adoption effort, vendor's track record with similar-size systems.
- *Industrial, user/champion persona:* A plant reliability engineer evaluating monitoring sensors; functional job = catch equipment failure before unplanned downtime; social job = be seen as the person who prevented the costly outage; decision criteria = installation disruption, alert accuracy (avoiding false positives), ruggedness for the plant environment.

## Common Mistakes

- Building personas around demographics/biography instead of jobs, pains, and decision criteria.
- Creating a persona for every job title encountered instead of consolidating to the handful of roles that materially change messaging or channel choice.
- Failing to distinguish which persona is the economic buyer versus the user/champion versus the technical evaluator/blocker, leading to messaging that talks past the actual decision-maker.
- Writing personas once at launch and never updating them as sales/support feedback accumulates.
- Treating persona work as separate from ICP work rather than nesting personas within the ICP(s) they belong to.

## Best Practices

- Anchor personas in JTBD (functional/emotional/social jobs) and a clear triggering situation.
- Limit to the minimum number of personas that meaningfully changes messaging, typically 2-4 per primary ICP.
- Explicitly name each persona's role in the buying committee (economic buyer, user/champion, technical evaluator, influencer, blocker).
- Ground persona detail in real interview/call/ticket data where possible; label hypothesis-based personas clearly and propose a validation step.
- Tie each persona directly to messaging pillars that answer its specific objections and decision criteria.

## Summary

Buyer personas represent the individual human roles inside a buying decision, distinct from the account-level ICP. The most useful personas are built on Jobs-To-Be-Done (functional, emotional, social) and a clear triggering situation, mapped to a specific role in the buying committee (economic buyer, user/champion, technical evaluator, influencer, blocker). Personas should be evidence-grounded where possible, limited to a focused set that changes messaging decisions, and directly linked to the messaging framework that addresses each persona's real objections and criteria.
