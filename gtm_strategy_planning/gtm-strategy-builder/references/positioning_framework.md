# Positioning Framework

**Purpose of this document:** Give Claude a rigorous, standard method for crafting or refreshing product/company positioning, independent of industry, so positioning output is defensible and differentiated rather than generic marketing language.

## Introduction

Positioning is the deliberate act of defining how a product or company should be perceived relative to alternatives, in the mind of a specific target buyer. Good positioning is not a tagline — it is a strategic decision about competitive frame of reference, differentiation, and target market that then informs messaging, sales narrative, and even product roadmap prioritization. This document synthesizes widely used positioning methodologies (Geoffrey Moore's competitive positioning approach and April Dunford's market-and-competitive-alternatives method) into a single generic framework.

## Detailed Explanation

### The Core Positioning Statement Structure
A complete positioning statement answers, in order:
1. **Target customer** — the specific segment/ICP this is for (not "everyone").
2. **Category / frame of reference** — what market or alternative set the buyer will mentally compare this to.
3. **Key differentiator** — the single most important way this is different from the frame of reference.
4. **Reason to believe (proof point)** — evidence that the differentiator is real and matters (data, capability, methodology, outcome).
5. **Value/benefit** — the outcome the differentiator produces for the target customer.

A standard template: *"For [target customer] who [need/problem], [product/company] is the [category] that [key differentiator], because [reason to believe] — unlike [primary alternative], which [alternative's limitation]."*

### Ten Components of Positioning (Dunford-style method)
A rigorous positioning exercise works through these components in sequence, because each depends on the one before it:
1. Competitive alternatives — what would the customer do/use if this didn't exist (including "do nothing" or a manual/legacy process, not just direct competitors).
2. Unique attributes/capabilities — what this has that alternatives lack.
3. Value/outcomes those attributes enable — translate features into customer-facing outcomes.
4. Target customer segment(s) that care most about that value — who values this most highly.
5. Market category — the frame of reference that makes the value obvious to that target customer.
6. Positioning statement — synthesize the above into the statement structure above.

### Category Design and Frame of Reference
Choosing the right category (frame of reference) is one of the highest-leverage positioning decisions. Three broad category strategies exist:
- **Compete in an existing, well-understood category** (fastest buyer comprehension, but you inherit the category leader's criteria for evaluation, which may not favor you).
- **Position as a leader/challenger within a category** (differentiate on a sub-attribute — e.g., "the fastest," "the most secure," "the easiest to deploy" — while keeping category comprehension intact).
- **Create or reframe a new category or sub-category** (higher long-term upside since you can define the evaluation criteria, but higher cost/time to educate the market and higher risk if the market does not adopt the new frame).

The right choice depends on market maturity, competitive intensity in the existing category, and the company's resources to sustain category-education marketing if a new category is chosen.

### Positioning vs. Messaging vs. Value Proposition
These three are related but distinct, and should not be treated as interchangeable:
- **Positioning** is the strategic decision (internal-facing, drives all downstream communication).
- **Value proposition** is the customer-facing articulation of the core benefit and its justification, typically a single clear statement (often the "elevator pitch" version of positioning).
- **Messaging** is the broader set of audience- and channel-specific communications derived from positioning (see `messaging_framework.md`), including pillars, proof points, and persona-specific variants.

## Professional Guidance

Positioning work should start from the competitive alternative and unique-attribute analysis — never start by writing a tagline. Validate the differentiator against real buyer criteria (what they actually evaluate on, ideally from win/loss data, see `competitive_analysis.md`) rather than what the company wishes were differentiating. Positioning should be re-validated, not rewritten from scratch, at each quarterly refresh unless the market, competitive set, or target segment has materially changed.

## Examples

- *Compete in existing category, differentiate on sub-attribute:* "For mid-market operations teams who need to track equipment health without hiring a data science team, [product] is the industrial monitoring platform that delivers plant-floor-ready alerts within a week of installation — unlike enterprise analytics suites, which require months of custom configuration before producing usable insight."
- *Category reframe:* A vendor selling automated compliance documentation software to healthcare providers might reframe from "compliance software" (a crowded, feature-compared category) to "continuous audit readiness," a category where its always-on evidence collection is the defining criterion rather than one of many checklist features.
- *Consumer example:* A direct-to-consumer meal-kit-adjacent service might position against "eating out" and "grocery shopping" as competitive alternatives rather than only against other meal-kit brands, widening the addressable frame of reference.

## Common Mistakes

- Writing positioning as a tagline exercise without first analyzing competitive alternatives and unique attributes.
- Claiming a differentiator that is not actually true or sustainable (easily copied, not defensible).
- Positioning against competitors only, ignoring "do nothing"/status quo/manual-process alternatives, which are often the real competition.
- Choosing a frame of reference (category) that the target customer does not actually use to evaluate purchases.
- Letting positioning drift silently as messaging is written ad hoc across teams, producing inconsistent external narratives.

## Best Practices

- Always name the competitive alternative set explicitly, including non-product alternatives (status quo, manual process, in-house build).
- Validate differentiators against actual buyer evaluation criteria, ideally sourced from win/loss interviews or sales feedback.
- Choose the frame of reference (category) deliberately, and state why it was chosen over alternative frames.
- Keep the positioning statement to one paragraph; if it requires a slide deck to explain, it is not yet positioning — it is still analysis.
- Re-validate positioning every quarter or at any major segment/competitive shift; avoid rewriting positioning reactively for every individual campaign.

## Summary

Positioning is a strategic, internally-facing decision built from competitive-alternative and unique-attribute analysis, expressed as a target customer + category + differentiator + reason to believe + value statement. The choice of category/frame of reference is one of the most consequential decisions in the process. Positioning is distinct from, and upstream of, both value proposition statements and full messaging frameworks, and should be periodically re-validated rather than rewritten from scratch each cycle.
