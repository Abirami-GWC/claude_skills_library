# Business Metrics Selection for Executive Audiences

**Purpose of this file:** Provide guidance on choosing the small set of metrics — leading vs. lagging indicators, north star metrics, and unit economics — appropriate for board and executive narratives, as opposed to the exhaustive dashboards used operationally.

## Introduction

One of the most common failures in executive communication is including too many metrics, or the wrong metrics, simply because they are available. A board or executive audience needs a small, curated set of numbers that answer "are we on track, and what does that mean for the decision at hand" — not the full operational dashboard. This reference explains how to select and present metrics for that audience: distinguishing leading from lagging indicators, identifying a north star metric, and summarizing unit economics at a glance.

## Detailed Explanation

### Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

- **Lagging indicators** measure outcomes that have already occurred — revenue, closed deals, churn realized, market share achieved. They are typically the metrics a board cares about most because they reflect actual results, but by definition they cannot be changed retroactively; they only confirm what has already happened.
- **Leading indicators** measure activity or conditions that are predictive of future lagging results — pipeline generated, engagement trends, conversion rate movement, early adoption signals. They matter to a board because they explain why a lagging result is likely to improve, worsen, or hold steady, and they are the metrics still within the organization's control to influence going forward.

A well-constructed executive metrics section pairs a small number of lagging results with the leading indicators that explain their trajectory — for example, pairing a current-quarter revenue result (lagging) with pipeline coverage or conversion trend (leading) to explain the outlook for the next period.

### North Star Metrics

A north star metric is the single measure that best captures the core value the business is delivering and that most other metrics ladder up to. Selecting a north star for a board narrative helps avoid a wall of disconnected numbers; every other metric shown should be explainable in terms of how it drives, or is driven by, the north star. The north star should be:
- Meaningful to the board without translation (revenue, active customer value, retained accounts, etc., depending on business model).
- Stable enough across cycles to allow trend comparison.
- Clearly connected to the strategic priorities being discussed, so the metric and the narrative reinforce each other.

### Unit Economics at a Glance

For any narrative involving investment asks, growth claims, or business model health, a compact unit economics view is often expected: what does it cost to acquire and serve a customer or account, and what value is returned over what time frame. At board altitude, this does not require a full financial model — it requires two or three ratios or figures (e.g., acquisition cost relative to value returned, payback period, retention rate) presented plainly, with trend, so the board can judge efficiency without needing a finance background.

### Selecting the Right Set

A useful rule of thumb: no more than five to seven metrics in a board-level narrative, chosen to answer three questions:
1. Are we winning or losing against the plan (lagging)?
2. Why, and is that likely to continue (leading)?
3. Is the underlying economics of the business improving or worsening (unit economics)?

Any metric that does not help answer one of these questions is a candidate for removal from the board-facing narrative, even if it remains useful operationally.

## Professional Guidance

- Always show trend (up/down/flat vs. prior period) and a comparison point (target, benchmark, or plan) alongside any metric — a number without context invites the board to ask for one.
- When metrics conflict (e.g., strong lagging results but weakening leading indicators), name the tension directly rather than presenting only the favorable number.
- Avoid vanity metrics (e.g., raw activity counts with no tie to outcome) in board materials even if they are tracked internally.
- When data is incomplete, state so explicitly rather than omitting the metric silently, especially if the board has asked about it previously.
- Reuse the same core metric set across cycles so trend lines are meaningful; introduce new metrics only when they represent a durable shift in what matters.

## Examples

**Overloaded metrics slide (avoid):** Fifteen metrics spanning channel-level activity, tool adoption rates, and team-level output, with no indication of which matter most.

**Well-selected metrics slide:** A north star metric with trend, one lagging result central to the current ask, two leading indicators explaining the trajectory, and a compact unit economics pairing (e.g., acquisition cost vs. value returned) — five figures total, each with trend and comparison point.

**Handling conflicting signals:** "Current-quarter results are ahead of plan (lagging), but conversion trend has softened over the last six weeks (leading) — we are treating this as an early risk to next quarter, not yet a change in trajectory."

## Common Mistakes

- Presenting an exhaustive operational dashboard instead of a curated, decision-relevant set.
- Showing metrics without trend or comparison, leaving the board unable to judge whether a number is good or bad.
- Including vanity or activity metrics that do not connect to outcomes.
- Selecting a different metric set every cycle, breaking the board's ability to track trend.
- Presenting only favorable metrics while omitting ones that contradict the narrative.

## Best Practices

- Cap board-facing metrics at five to seven, chosen around a single north star.
- Pair every lagging metric shown with its explanatory leading indicator where possible.
- Keep the metric set stable across cycles; version changes deliberately and note when a change is made.
- Present unit economics as two or three plain ratios, not a full model.
- Name conflicting or ambiguous signals directly rather than only showing the flattering half of the picture.

## Summary

Choosing the right metrics for a board or executive audience is a curation exercise: pair a small number of lagging results with the leading indicators that explain them, anchor the set around a single north star metric, and summarize unit economics in a glance rather than a full model. A well-selected metric set tells the board what happened, why, and whether the underlying business is getting healthier — without burying them in an operational dashboard.
