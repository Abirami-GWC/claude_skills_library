# Decision Frameworks for Board Asks

**Purpose of this file:** Provide structures for framing any ask made of a board — options analysis, recommendation-with-rationale structuring, and risk-adjusted framing — so requests are decision-ready rather than open-ended.

## Introduction

Whenever a board narrative includes an ask — approving a budget increase, endorsing a pivot, signing off on a strategic priority — the ask must be structured so the board can evaluate and act on it efficiently. An ask presented without alternatives, without a clear recommendation, or without risk framing forces the board to do analytical work that should have been done before the material was presented. This reference covers three structures this skill applies to any ask: options analysis, recommendation-with-rationale, and risk-adjusted framing.

## Detailed Explanation

### Options Analysis

Every ask should be presented alongside at least one credible alternative, even if the alternative is simply "do nothing" or "maintain current course." Options analysis typically includes:
- **The recommended option** — what is being asked for.
- **At least one alternative** — a genuinely considered different path, not a strawman included only to make the recommendation look better.
- **The status quo / do-nothing option** — useful as a baseline, especially for asks involving new investment, since it clarifies the cost of inaction.

For each option, briefly note the expected outcome, the cost or resource implication, and the primary risk. This does not need to be exhaustive; two to three options with a few lines each is typically sufficient at board altitude.

### Recommendation-with-Rationale Structuring

After laying out options, state the recommendation explicitly and support it with rationale that connects back to the options analysis:
- **Recommendation** — the specific ask, stated as a decision the board can approve (e.g., "approve an incremental [X] in budget for [purpose] over [timeframe]").
- **Rationale** — why this option was chosen over the alternatives, referencing the tradeoffs surfaced in the options analysis (cost, speed, risk, strategic fit).
- **What happens if approved** and **what happens if not approved** — stating the consequence of each path helps the board understand the stakes of the decision, not just the mechanics of it.

### Risk-Adjusted Framing

Every ask carries risk, and boards are typically responsible for weighing that risk explicitly. Risk-adjusted framing means:
- Naming the specific risks associated with the recommended option (not just generic caveats).
- Stating the business impact of each risk if it materializes, not just a technical description.
- Noting any mitigation already planned or available.
- Where relevant, expressing the ask in terms of downside exposure versus upside opportunity, so the board can weigh the decision as a risk/return tradeoff rather than a binary yes/no.

A risk-adjusted ask reads differently from an unadjusted one: instead of "we recommend X," it reads "we recommend X; the primary risk is Y, with an estimated impact of Z if it materializes, which we plan to mitigate by [action]; the alternative of not proceeding carries its own risk of [cost of inaction]."

## Professional Guidance

- Never present a single-option ask to a board; always show at least the status quo as a comparison point.
- State the recommendation before the supporting rationale, consistent with BLUF discipline (see `executive_communication.md`).
- Quantify risk and opportunity wherever data allows; where it does not, use clear qualitative bands (e.g., high/medium/low impact) rather than vague language.
- Make the cost of inaction explicit for any investment ask — boards often underweight the risk of not acting unless it is named directly.
- Keep the options analysis proportionate to the size of the ask; a small budget reallocation does not need the same depth of options analysis as a strategic pivot.

## Examples

**Underspecified ask (avoid):** "We would like to increase the budget for this initiative."

**Well-structured ask:** "We recommend approving a [X]% increase to the [initiative] budget for the next two quarters (recommendation). We considered three options: maintaining current spend (status quo), the proposed increase, and a larger increase focused on faster expansion. The proposed level balances expected return against execution risk better than either alternative (rationale). The primary risk is slower-than-expected payback if [condition]; we will monitor [leading indicator] monthly and can scale back if it underperforms (risk-adjusted framing). Declining to increase spend risks ceding the current growth trajectory to competitive pressure (cost of inaction)."

**Pivot ask (well-structured):** "We recommend shifting strategic focus from [prior approach] to [new approach] (recommendation), having evaluated continuing the current approach, pausing to reassess, and this pivot; the pivot best addresses the ceiling we have observed (rationale). Key risk: execution disruption during transition, mitigated by a phased rollout over [timeframe] (risk-adjusted framing)."

## Common Mistakes

- Presenting an ask with no alternative considered, leaving the board unable to judge whether the recommendation is well-reasoned.
- Stating risk only in vague terms ("there is some risk") without business impact or magnitude.
- Omitting the cost of inaction, understating the case for acting.
- Burying the recommendation after a long options discussion instead of stating it up front.
- Failing to state clearly what happens if the board does or does not approve the ask.

## Best Practices

- Always include at least a status-quo comparison alongside the recommended option.
- State the recommendation first, then the rationale, consistent with BLUF.
- Quantify or clearly band the risk and opportunity associated with the recommendation.
- Make the cost of inaction explicit for any investment or change request.
- Scale the depth of options analysis to the size and reversibility of the decision.

## Summary

A well-framed board ask presents genuine options (including the status quo), states a clear recommendation with rationale tied to those options, and frames risk in terms of business impact alongside the cost of inaction. Applying these three structures consistently turns an open-ended request into a decision the board can efficiently evaluate and act on.
