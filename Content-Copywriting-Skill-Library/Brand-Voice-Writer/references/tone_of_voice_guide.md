# Tone of Voice Guide

## Purpose

This document explains how tone (the contextual flex of a brand's constant voice) should shift across situations, channels, and emotional registers, and gives Claude a practical method for calibrating it.

## Introduction

Voice is constant; tone is situational. A brand with a witty, energetic voice should not sound witty in a data-breach notification or a subscription-cancellation confirmation. Tone mismanagement — being upbeat when a customer is frustrated, or overly formal in a casual social reply — is one of the fastest ways content reads as off-brand, even when vocabulary and personality are technically correct.

## Detailed Explanation

### The Tone Matrix

Cross the brand's core personality against the emotional state of the moment:

| Situation type | Tone adjustment |
|---|---|
| Celebration (launch, milestone, win) | Lean into full brand personality — highest energy point |
| Everyday informational (how-to, product update) | Baseline brand voice, no dial-up or dial-down |
| Customer frustration / complaint | Dial down playfulness and humor sharply; prioritize clarity, empathy, ownership |
| Error / failure state | Direct, honest, no jokes at the user's expense; brand personality shows in helpfulness, not humor |
| Legal / compliance / security | Formality increases regardless of baseline voice; clarity and precision override personality expression |
| Sales / persuasion | Confidence and energy rise; still bounded by honesty norms |
| Bereavement, layoffs, sensitive news | Personality recedes almost entirely; plain, respectful, human language |

### Tone Sliders

For any piece, ask:
1. How urgent/serious is this moment for the reader?
2. Is the reader currently frustrated, neutral, or delighted?
3. What's the cost of getting the tone wrong here (a joke in a security alert can damage trust; overly serious language in a celebratory tweet can feel flat)?

Higher stakes and higher reader frustration both push tone toward more restrained, clear, and empathetic — regardless of what the "fun" baseline voice is.

## Professional Guidance

- When in doubt, err toward clarity and respect over personality expression — brands are rarely punished for being slightly too plain in a serious moment, but often are for being flippant in one.
- Empathy language ("we know this is frustrating," "here's exactly what happens next") is compatible with almost any brand voice and should be used generously in negative-state communications.
- Humor should never target the reader, even gently, in a moment of their frustration or failure.
- Tone shifts should be gradual within a single piece, not jarring — don't open playful and close somber without a clear narrative reason.

## Examples

**Baseline voice:** Playful, energetic
**Celebration tone:** "We did it. 100,000 users and counting — thank you for building this with us. 🎉"
**Error-state tone (same brand):** "Something went wrong on our end. Your data is safe, and we're already on it. Try again in a few minutes, or reach out and we'll sort it out directly."

Notice: the error-state message drops the emoji and exclamation energy but keeps the direct, human "we've got you" quality that is the brand's real personality underneath the playfulness.

## Common Mistakes

- Using identical energy/humor level in a support/error context as in marketing content.
- Treating "professional" contexts (legal, security) as requiring a totally different, generic voice rather than the same voice at lower energy.
- Losing all brand personality in negative-state communication, making it read as a form letter from any company.
- Escalating tone too aggressively in sales contexts to the point of overpromising or pressuring language.

## Best Practices

- Map the situation to the tone matrix before drafting.
- Keep a "tone floor" — even at the most restrained end, some minimal brand personality marker should remain so the piece is still recognizably the brand.
- Read the piece back as the intended reader in their likely emotional state, not as a neutral observer.

## Summary

Tone is voice adjusted for context. Use a tone matrix mapping situation type to energy/formality level, always prioritize clarity and empathy in negative or high-stakes moments, and keep enough of the underlying personality present that restrained copy still feels like the same brand.
