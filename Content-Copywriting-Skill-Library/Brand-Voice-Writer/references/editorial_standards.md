# Editorial & Grammar Standards

## Purpose

This document defines the baseline editorial, grammar, and consistency standards Claude applies to all Brand Voice Writer output, independent of any specific brand's voice — the "floor" of quality every deliverable must meet.

## Introduction

Brand voice governs style; editorial standards govern correctness and consistency. A piece can be perfectly on-voice and still be unpublishable if it has grammar errors, inconsistent terminology, or formatting problems. This reference exists so those baseline standards aren't re-litigated per brand.

## Detailed Explanation

### Grammar & Mechanics

- Subject-verb agreement, correct tense, and consistent point of view (first/second/third person) throughout a piece unless the brand voice explicitly varies it intentionally (e.g., switching to second person for a direct CTA).
- Oxford comma usage should follow the brand's stated preference; if unstated, default to using it for clarity and stay consistent throughout the piece.
- One space after sentence-ending punctuation.
- Numbers: spell out zero through nine in prose; use numerals for 10+, unless brand style dictates otherwise (common in marketing copy to use numerals for emphasis, e.g., "3 steps").

### Consistency

- Terminology: once a term is chosen for a concept (e.g., "sign in" vs. "log in"), use it consistently within a document and ideally across a batch of documents.
- Capitalization: product/feature names should follow the capitalization established in source material or brand guide exactly.
- Formatting: consistent use of headers, bullet vs. numbered lists (numbered for sequential steps, bullets for non-sequential items), and consistent heading capitalization style (sentence case vs. title case).

### Clarity

- Prefer active voice unless passive voice is stylistically intentional (e.g., de-emphasizing an actor: "mistakes were made" vs. brand-voice-appropriate directness).
- Avoid unnecessary jargon unless the audience is expert and the brand voice embraces technical language.
- Avoid stacked prepositional phrases and buried verbs ("we have the ability to provide assistance" → "we can help").
- Keep sentence length varied — long unbroken paragraphs of uniform sentence length read as monotonous regardless of voice.

## Professional Guidance

- Editorial correctness should never be sacrificed for voice — a brand voice that is "casual" still needs to be grammatically coherent; casual is a style choice about contractions and sentence structure, not license for errors.
- When source material contains an established but non-standard convention (e.g., a stylized product name with unusual capitalization), preserve it exactly as the source has it.
- Flag ambiguous editorial choices (e.g., serial comma policy) to the user once, rather than silently guessing differently each time.

## Examples

**Before (buried verb, passive, wordy):** "An assessment of the situation was undertaken by our team in order to provide a resolution."
**After (clear, active):** "Our team looked into it and found a fix."

## Common Mistakes

- Inconsistent terminology within the same document (calling the same feature two different names).
- Mixing tenses or point of view without stylistic reason.
- Treating "casual voice" as permission for grammatical looseness that reduces clarity.
- Inconsistent list formatting (mixing bullets and numbers arbitrarily) within one piece.

## Best Practices

- Run a terminology consistency pass after drafting, especially on longer or multi-piece deliverables.
- Read content aloud (or simulate doing so) to catch buried verbs and awkward rhythm.
- Preserve any established brand-specific spelling/capitalization exactly.

## Summary

Editorial standards are the non-negotiable floor beneath brand voice: correct grammar, consistent terminology and formatting, and clear, active writing — regardless of how casual, playful, or formal the brand's voice is.
