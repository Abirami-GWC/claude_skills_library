# Glossary

## Purpose

Defines the key terms used across this skill's documents so terminology stays consistent between SKILL.md, references, templates, prompts, and examples.

## Introduction

Copywriting and brand strategy terms are often used loosely or inconsistently across teams. This glossary anchors a shared, precise vocabulary for this skill.

## Detailed Explanation / Terms

- **Brand voice** — the constant, defining personality and style of a brand's communication, expressed through personality, tone, language, structure, and point of view.
- **Tone** — the contextual adjustment of brand voice based on situation, audience emotional state, and channel.
- **Messaging pillar** — a recurring substantive theme or value proposition the brand emphasizes across content.
- **Positioning statement** — a concise articulation of what the brand is, for whom, and why it matters, relative to alternatives.
- **Proof point** — a specific fact, feature, or piece of evidence supporting a messaging pillar.
- **Voice fingerprint** — a shorthand internal reference (dimension placements + do/don't examples) used to keep voice consistent across a long or multi-part deliverable.
- **Terminology consistency** — the practice of using the same word/phrase for the same concept throughout a document or content set.
- **Style guide** — a documented set of rules covering voice, tone, grammar, and formatting for a brand.
- **On-voice / off-voice** — shorthand for whether a piece of copy matches or diverges from the defined brand voice.
- **Register** — the level of formality of language used in a given piece or context.

## Professional Guidance

Use these terms precisely in explanations to the user — e.g., say "this is a tone issue, not a voice issue" when a piece is contextually mismatched but otherwise on-brand, since the fix differs.

## Examples

"The vocabulary and sentence structure are on-voice, but the tone is off — this apology email reads too upbeat for the context."

## Common Mistakes

- Using "tone" and "voice" interchangeably, which obscures whether the fix needed is contextual (tone) or fundamental (voice).
- Calling any factual inconsistency a "voice" problem when it is actually a messaging or accuracy problem.

## Best Practices

Use precise terminology when giving feedback or rationale to users so they can build their own internal understanding of their brand voice over time.

## Summary

A shared, precise vocabulary — voice, tone, pillar, positioning, proof point, register — prevents miscommunication when discussing brand copy with users and across this skill's documentation.
