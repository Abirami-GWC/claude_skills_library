# Subject Line Playbook

## Purpose
A reference for writing subject lines and preview text that earn opens without resorting to clickbait or spam triggers.

## Introduction
The subject line's only job is to earn the open; it doesn't need to summarize the whole email. Preview text is the second sentence of that same pitch, never a repeat of the subject.

## Detailed Explanation

### Proven Subject Line Patterns
- **Curiosity gap**: "The mistake most people make with X" — implies a specific, resolvable answer inside.
- **Direct benefit**: "Cut your onboarding time in half" — clear value stated plainly.
- **Personal/conversational**: "quick question" or "can I ask you something?" — feels like a 1:1 email, works well in nurture sequences.
- **Numbers/specificity**: "3 things I wish I knew before launching" — specificity builds credibility.
- **Urgency (only when real)**: "Doors close Friday at midnight" — must correspond to an actual deadline.
- **Story hook**: "I almost gave up on this" — invites narrative curiosity.

### Preview Text Rules
- Extend the subject line's idea; never restate it.
- Keep to 35-90 characters (varies by client) — front-load the most important words.
- Avoid "View this email in your browser" boilerplate as the visible preview.

### Length and Formatting
- Optimal subject length: 30-50 characters for mobile-heavy audiences.
- Sentence case reads as more human than Title Case; avoid ALL CAPS.
- Limit to one emoji, only if on-brand; omit for B2B/professional contexts.
- Avoid spam-trigger words in excess: "free," "guarantee," "act now," "$$$," excessive exclamation points.

## Examples
- Weak: "NEWSLETTER #47 - CHECK IT OUT!!!"
- Strong: "the one metric most founders ignore"
- Weak: "50% OFF EVERYTHING - LIMITED TIME - ACT NOW!!!"
- Strong: "your cart's about to expire (24 hrs left)"

## Common Mistakes
- Subject line promises something the email doesn't deliver
- Preview text just repeats the subject line word-for-word
- Overusing urgency until it stops being believable
- Same subject line style repeated across every email in a sequence, causing pattern fatigue

## Best Practices
- Write 3 subject line variants per email, pick the strongest, keep one alternate for A/B testing.
- Read the subject line out loud — if it sounds like ad copy rather than a person, rewrite it.
- Vary subject line style across a sequence (question, statement, curiosity, direct) to avoid predictability.

## Summary
Good subject lines are specific, human, and honest about what's inside; preview text is a second, complementary hook — not a repeat.
