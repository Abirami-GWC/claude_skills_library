# Email Sequence Frameworks

## Purpose
Give Claude a library of proven multi-email architectures so a sequence's length, order, and per-email purpose don't have to be invented from scratch each time.

## Introduction
Every email sequence is a small story with a beginning, middle, and end, aimed at moving a specific audience from one state to another. This document catalogs standard architectures for the most common sequence types, each with a recommended email count, cadence, and per-email purpose.

## Detailed Explanation & Frameworks

### 1. Welcome Series (Subscriber to Engaged Reader)
Standard length: 4-5 emails over 7-10 days.
1. Immediate welcome (send instantly): Deliver on the promise that got them to subscribe. Set expectations.
2. Story/credibility (day 2): Who you are, why you do this, why they should trust you.
3. Value/best content (day 4): Your single best piece of content or insight, no pitch.
4. Social proof (day 6): Testimonials, results, community.
5. Soft CTA (day 9): Invite the next logical step, low pressure.

### 2. Lead Nurture (Cold Lead to Sales-Ready)
Standard length: 5-7 emails over 2-3 weeks.
1. Deliver the promised resource.
2. Address the #1 audience problem (educational, no pitch).
3. Reframe the problem: show the cost of inaction.
4. Case study / proof it's solvable.
5. Introduce the solution as the answer.
6. Handle the top objection.
7. Direct offer with a clear next step.

### 3. Onboarding (New User to Activated User)
Standard length: 4-6 emails, triggered by signup and usage milestones, not just days.
1. Welcome + single first action.
2. Day 2-3: core feature tied to their stated goal.
3. Day 5: common early confusion or a quick win.
4. Day 7: social proof / what power users do differently.
5. Day 10-14: check-in, surface support resources.
6. Trial-ending or milestone email: upgrade/next-step CTA.

### 4. Re-engagement / Win-back (Dormant to Reactivated)
Standard length: 3-4 emails over 1-2 weeks.
1. "We miss you," low-key and curiosity-driven, no discount yet.
2. What's changed since they left; remove the reason they churned.
3. Genuine incentive or discount.
4. Final "break-up" email: honest tone, list-cleaning CTA.

### 5. Cart Abandonment
Standard length: 3 emails over 24-72 hours.
1. Reminder (1-3 hrs): simple, helpful, "did you forget something?"
2. Objection handling (24 hrs): shipping/returns/trust concerns, social proof.
3. Incentive/urgency (48-72 hrs): discount or urgency, final nudge.

### 6. Post-Purchase / Retention
Standard length: 3-5 emails over 2-4 weeks.
1. Order confirmation warmth + what to expect.
2. Usage/onboarding tips for the product.
3. Review/feedback request after adequate use time.
4. Complementary product / replenishment reminder.
5. Loyalty/referral invitation.

### 7. Product Launch / Promotional
Standard length: 4-6 emails over 5-10 days.
1. Announcement/teaser: build anticipation, no full details.
2. Full reveal: features, benefits, founding story.
3. Social proof / early access results.
4. Objection handling / FAQ.
5. Urgency: deadline approaching.
6. Last call: final hours.

## Professional Guidance
Match sequence length to relationship depth: cold audiences need more trust-building before any ask; existing customers can move faster. Trigger-based timing generally outperforms pure calendar-based timing when the ESP supports it. Never skip the early no-pitch trust-building email(s) in cold sequences.

## Examples
A SaaS onboarding sequence tied to product usage milestones typically shows higher activation than one driven purely by days-since-signup, because each email lands when it is most relevant to what the user is actually doing.

## Common Mistakes
Front-loading the sales pitch before trust exists; sending on a fixed calendar regardless of behavior; repeating the same CTA with no escalation; padding sequences longer than the goal requires.

## Best Practices
Give every email one clear job no other email in the sequence also does. Shorten sequences when the offer doesn't justify standard length. End nurture/win-back sequences with a clean exit path.

## Summary
Choose the architecture matching the audience's starting relationship to the brand, use trigger-based timing where possible, and give each email a distinct, non-overlapping job.
