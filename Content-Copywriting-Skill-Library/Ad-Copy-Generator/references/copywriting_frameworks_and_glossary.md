# Copywriting Frameworks & Glossary

## Purpose

Documents classic direct-response copywriting structures used to organize full ad body copy (beyond just the headline), plus a glossary of terms used across this skill's documents.

## Introduction

Beyond a strong hook, the body of an ad benefits from a coherent persuasive structure. This reference gives Claude several proven frameworks to structure body copy, and a shared vocabulary for consistent terminology.

## Detailed Explanation

### Body Copy Frameworks

1. **AIDA** — Attention (hook) → Interest (elaborate the problem/desire) → Desire (paint the outcome/benefit) → Action (CTA). General-purpose, works across most channels.
2. **PAS** — Problem → Agitate (make the cost of the problem vivid) → Solution. Strong for pain-avoidance-driven offers.
3. **FAB** — Feature → Advantage → Benefit. Useful for more technical/B2B audiences who want to understand *how* something works, not just the emotional payoff.
4. **Before/After/Bridge** — Paint the reader's current state → their desired state → the product as the bridge between them. Strong for transformation-oriented offers (fitness, productivity, financial).
5. **4 Ps (Promise/Picture/Proof/Push)** — Promise the outcome → Picture what it looks like → Proof it's real (real data/testimonials only) → Push with a clear CTA.

### Choosing a Framework

| Context | Best-fit framework |
|---|---|
| Pain-point-driven consumer offer | PAS |
| Transformation/aspirational offer | Before/After/Bridge |
| Technical/B2B audience wanting specifics | FAB |
| General-purpose/short format | AIDA |
| Offer with strong, real proof available | 4 Ps |

## Glossary

- **Hook** — the opening line/headline designed to capture attention.
- **Angle** — the specific psychological or positioning approach an ad variation takes (e.g., "time-saving angle" vs. "cost-saving angle" for the same product).
- **Funnel stage** — where the audience is in their buying journey: cold/awareness, consideration, decision, retargeting.
- **Primary text** — the main body copy field in feed-based ad formats (e.g., Meta), typically shown above the creative.
- **CTA (call to action)** — the specific instruction/button prompting the reader's next action.
- **Value proposition** — the core benefit/reason the offer matters to the audience, stated clearly.
- **Dark pattern** — a manipulative design or copy tactic (fake urgency, hidden terms, disguised billing) that this skill will not produce.
- **A/B variation** — a distinct version of an ad created to test against others, ideally differing in angle, not just wording.

## Professional Guidance

- Choose the framework based on offer type and audience sophistication, not by default habit.
- Combine frameworks when useful (e.g., PAS for the hook/body, then a 4 Ps-style proof-and-push close).
- Keep terminology precise and consistent when discussing strategy with the user (e.g., "angle" vs. just "variation" — an angle changes the underlying approach, not just the wording).

## Examples

**PAS example (fitness app):**
- Problem: "Most people quit their fitness routine within 3 weeks."
- Agitate: "Not because they're lazy — because generic plans don't fit their actual life."
- Solution: "Our app builds a plan around your real schedule, so it's still doable in week 6."

## Common Mistakes

- Using AIDA by default without considering whether PAS or Before/After/Bridge would better fit the specific offer.
- Mixing frameworks incoherently within a single short ad (e.g., agitating a problem, then pivoting to unrelated features without a clear bridge).

## Best Practices

- Match framework to offer type and audience sophistication level.
- Keep each variation coherent within its chosen framework rather than mixing structures messily.

## Summary

AIDA, PAS, FAB, Before/After/Bridge, and 4 Ps are the core body-copy frameworks to draw from, matched to offer type and audience. A shared glossary (hook, angle, funnel stage, CTA, value proposition, dark pattern) keeps terminology consistent across this skill's documentation.
