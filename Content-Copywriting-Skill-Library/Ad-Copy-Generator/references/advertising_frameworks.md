# Advertising Frameworks & Channel Specifications

## Purpose

Documents the structural conventions and general character-limit ranges for major ad channels so generated copy is channel-appropriate and usable without extensive reformatting. (Platforms periodically change exact limits — treat these as reliable general guidance, and note to the user that they should verify current exact limits in the ad platform itself before final launch.)

## Introduction

Ad copy that ignores channel format wastes the user's time — a Meta primary-text-length hook does not fit a Google Search headline field. This reference keeps generated copy structurally realistic per channel.

## Detailed Explanation

### Google Search Ads
- Multiple short headlines (~30 characters each) that rotate/combine, plus longer description lines (~90 characters each).
- Convention: front-load the value proposition/keyword in the first headline; use additional headlines for secondary benefits, offers, or credibility (guarantees, social proof).
- Intent-driven audience — copy should assume the reader already searched for something related; skip curiosity-gap games in favor of direct relevance.

### Google Display Ads
- Very limited space (short headline, short description, sometimes a long headline).
- Visual-first format — copy should be minimal and complementary to imagery, not carry the whole message alone.

### Meta (Facebook/Instagram) Ads
- Primary text (above the image/video) carries the hook — front-load the strongest line since feeds truncate after a couple of lines ("See more").
- Headline (below the image) is short and benefit- or offer-focused.
- Works well with conversational, scroll-stopping hooks (curiosity, problem-agitate, identity-naming).

### LinkedIn Ads
- More professional register expected even in a "bold" brand voice.
- Primary text similar structure to Meta but audience typically responds better to credibility, specificity, and outcome-focused copy over casual curiosity hooks.
- CTA button copy is typically from a fixed platform list (e.g., "Learn more," "Sign up," "Download") — note this constraint to the user.

### Product/Shopping Ads
- Minimal copy space; title and short description should be highly specific (product name, key differentiator, price/offer if applicable) rather than persuasive prose.

## Professional Guidance

- Always ask or confirm the channel before drafting extensively, since character limits differ enough to make untargeted copy unusable.
- Note to the user that exact platform character limits and CTA button options can change — recommend a quick platform check before final launch, since this reference reflects general, stable conventions rather than a live spec.
- Front-load the strongest line/value proposition on every channel — regardless of format, the first several words carry disproportionate weight.

## Examples

**Google Search headline set (3 headlines, ~30 chars each):**
1. "Invoice Software for Freelancers"
2. "Get Paid 2 Weeks Faster"
3. "Free 14-Day Trial, No Card"

**Meta primary text (front-loaded hook):**
"Still chasing clients for payment? Our freelancers get paid an average of 12 days faster — see how in under 2 minutes."

## Common Mistakes

- Writing Meta-style long curiosity hooks for Google Search ads, where intent-matching directness performs better.
- Ignoring LinkedIn's more fixed CTA button list and writing custom CTA copy that won't actually appear as written.
- Treating Display ad space as if it had room for a full pitch.

## Best Practices

- Confirm channel before drafting.
- Front-load value proposition regardless of channel.
- Flag to the user that platform specs should be double-checked before launch, since these change periodically.

## Summary

Each ad channel has distinct structural conventions and space constraints; matching copy structure and register to the specific channel — while always front-loading the core value proposition — produces usable, effective ad copy.
