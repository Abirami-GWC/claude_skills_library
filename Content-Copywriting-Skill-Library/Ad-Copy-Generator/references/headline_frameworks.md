# Headline & Hook Frameworks

## Purpose

Gives Claude a structured set of proven headline/hook frameworks to draw from when generating ad copy, so variations represent genuinely distinct, tested angles rather than superficial rewordings.

## Introduction

The headline or opening line (the "hook") is the single highest-leverage element of any ad — it determines whether the rest of the copy gets read at all. Strong ad copy generation relies on drawing from a set of distinct, proven structural patterns rather than free-associating.

## Detailed Explanation

### Core Headline Frameworks

1. **Direct benefit** — states the core value proposition plainly. *"Cut your invoicing time in half."*
2. **Curiosity gap** — implies there's information the reader wants, without giving it away. *"The reason your ads stop converting after 2 weeks."*
3. **Problem-agitate** — names the reader's specific pain point directly. *"Still tracking expenses in spreadsheets?"*
4. **Social proof lead** — leads with numbers/popularity. *"Join 40,000 teams who ditched spreadsheets."* (Only use real, user-supplied numbers.)
5. **Question hook** — engages the reader by asking something they'd answer "yes" or "no" to, that primes the pitch. *"Tired of chasing invoices?"*
6. **How-to / outcome** — promises a specific transformation. *"How to get paid 2 weeks faster, without awkward follow-ups."*
7. **Contrarian / pattern interrupt** — challenges a common assumption. *"Your CRM isn't the problem. Your follow-up process is."*
8. **Specificity/number lead** — uses a precise, credible number instead of a vague superlative. *"Set up in 11 minutes, not 11 days."*
9. **Urgency/timeliness** (use only with genuine cause) — ties to a real deadline, season, or event.
10. **Identity/audience-naming** — directly names who the ad is for. *"Built for solo consultants who hate admin work."*

### Matching Framework to Funnel Stage

| Funnel stage | Best-fit frameworks |
|---|---|
| Cold / awareness | Curiosity gap, problem-agitate, contrarian, identity-naming |
| Consideration | Direct benefit, how-to/outcome, specificity |
| Retargeting / bottom funnel | Social proof, urgency (if genuine), direct benefit + guarantee/risk-reversal |

### Matching Framework to Channel

- **Google Search:** Direct benefit and specificity perform well — searchers already have intent; don't waste characters on curiosity games.
- **Meta/Instagram:** Curiosity gap, problem-agitate, and identity-naming work well in a scroll-stopping feed context.
- **LinkedIn:** Direct benefit, specificity, and professional identity-naming; contrarian works well for thought-leadership-adjacent B2B ads but should stay credible, not clickbait-y.
- **Display:** Very short — direct benefit or specificity almost always beats curiosity, since there's little room to pay off a curiosity gap.

## Professional Guidance

- Always generate variations from at least 2-3 *different* frameworks per request, not 5 wordings of the same framework — this is what makes A/B testing meaningful.
- Curiosity gaps must still be honest — the payoff in the body copy must match what the hook implied, or it reads as clickbait and damages trust.
- Specificity beats vagueness: "10 minutes" beats "fast," "40,000 teams" beats "thousands of teams" (when true).

## Examples

**Product:** Expense-tracking app for freelancers
- Direct benefit: "Track every expense in one tap."
- Curiosity gap: "The expense mistake costing freelancers thousands at tax time."
- Problem-agitate: "Still digging through your inbox for receipts every March?"
- Identity-naming: "Built for freelancers who'd rather work than do bookkeeping."

## Common Mistakes

- Generating 5 headlines that are all the same framework reworded (fails to produce a genuine test).
- Curiosity gaps that overpromise relative to what the ad/landing page actually delivers.
- Using vague superlatives ("the best," "amazing," "revolutionary") instead of concrete specifics.
- Ignoring channel norms (long curiosity-building copy on a Display ad with an 8-word limit).

## Best Practices

- Pair each headline with a distinct emotional or logical angle, not just different phrasing.
- Always ground specificity claims (numbers, timeframes) in information the user actually provided.
- Keep the funnel stage in mind — don't lead cold-traffic ads with heavy social proof before the audience knows what the product is.

## Summary

Ten proven headline/hook frameworks, matched to funnel stage and channel, give a systematic way to generate genuinely distinct, testable ad variations rather than superficial rewordings of one idea.
