# Headline Frameworks

## Purpose
Structural patterns for writing hero headlines and subheadlines that pass the 5-second clarity test.

## Introduction
The hero headline is the single highest-leverage line on a landing page — most visitors decide whether to keep reading based on it alone.

## Detailed Explanation

### Proven Headline Patterns
- **Outcome-first**: "[Achieve specific outcome] without [common pain point]" — e.g., "Ship features faster without adding headcount."
- **Direct value statement**: "[Product] helps [audience] [do specific thing]" — clear and literal, good for unfamiliar categories.
- **Question hook**: "Tired of [specific pain point]?" — works when the pain is universally recognized by the audience.
- **Number/specificity**: "[Number] [outcome] in [timeframe]" — e.g., "3x your reply rate in 30 days."
- **Contrarian/curiosity**: "The [common belief] is wrong — here's what actually works" — use sparingly, must be backed up immediately.

### Subheadline's Job
The subheadline supports the headline with a concrete mechanism or clarification — how the outcome happens, or who it's for — never just a rephrasing of the headline.

## Examples
- Headline: "Stop losing leads to slow follow-up."
- Subheadline: "AutoReply responds to every new lead in under 60 seconds, automatically."

## Common Mistakes
- Headlines that are clever but unclear about what the product actually does
- Subheadlines that just restate the headline in different words
- Leading with the company name or a generic tagline instead of a benefit

## Best Practices
- Write the headline last, after knowing the full page's core argument, then compress that argument into one line.
- Generate 3 headline variants using different patterns before selecting the strongest.
- Read the headline out of context — a stranger should understand what's offered and for whom within 5 seconds.

## Summary
A strong hero headline states a clear, specific outcome for a specific audience; the subheadline adds the mechanism or clarification, never a repeat.
