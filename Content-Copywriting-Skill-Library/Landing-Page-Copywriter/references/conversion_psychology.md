# Conversion Psychology

## Purpose
Core psychological principles that inform section ordering, proof placement, and objection handling on a landing page.

## Introduction
Landing page conversion is less about clever wording and more about removing friction and uncertainty at each point a visitor might hesitate.

## Detailed Explanation

### Principles
- **Clarity beats persuasion.** A confused visitor never converts, no matter how persuasive the copy. Clarity is the first job of every section.
- **Proof must sit next to claims.** An unsupported benefit claim reads as marketing fluff; the same claim next to a testimonial or stat reads as credible.
- **Loss aversion vs. gain framing.** Both work; loss framing ("stop losing X") tends to work well for pain-driven categories, gain framing ("achieve X") for aspirational categories.
- **Reciprocity in lead-gen pages.** Offering genuine value up front (free tool, guide) before asking for the email increases opt-in rates.
- **Social proof specificity.** "10,000+ customers" is weaker than "10,482 businesses, including [recognizable name]." Specific numbers read as more credible than round ones.
- **Risk reversal.** Guarantees, free trials, and easy cancellation reduce the perceived risk of the ask and are especially important for higher-price offers.
- **Progressive disclosure.** Address the visitor's most basic question first (what is this?) before more advanced objections (how does pricing work?) — mirrors natural decision-making order.

## Examples
A high-price course page that adds a specific, named case study directly beneath its biggest outcome claim will generally out-convert the same page with only a vague "life-changing!" pull quote.

## Common Mistakes
- Burying proof at the bottom of the page instead of next to the claims it supports
- Using vague, rounded statistics instead of specific numbers
- Skipping risk reversal (no guarantee/trial mentioned) on a high-price offer

## Best Practices
- For every major claim, ask "what proves this?" and place that proof nearby.
- Order objection-handling from most fundamental (what is this, is it for me) to most specific (pricing, technical details).
- Use risk-reversal language prominently near the primary CTA on higher-commitment offers.

## Summary
Conversion is driven by clarity, proof placed next to claims, and reduced perceived risk — not just persuasive adjectives.
