# Landing Page Frameworks

## Purpose
A reference for standard landing page architectures by page type, so section order and length don't have to be reinvented per request.

## Introduction
Different offers require different page structures and lengths. This document defines the three most common landing page types and their standard section order.

## Detailed Explanation

### 1. Short-Form SaaS / Product Landing Page
Best for: low-to-mid consideration software or digital products with a free trial or low entry price.
Sections: Hero (headline/subheadline/CTA) → Logo bar/social proof → 3-column benefits → Feature highlight with screenshot description → Testimonial → Pricing/CTA → FAQ → Final CTA.

### 2. Long-Form Sales Page
Best for: high-consideration, high-price, or unfamiliar offers (courses, coaching, high-ticket services).
Sections: Hero (big promise) → Problem agitation → Story/credibility → Solution introduction → Detailed benefits (not just features) → Social proof (multiple testimonials/case studies) → What's included/curriculum → Objection handling/FAQ → Guarantee → Pricing → Final CTA with urgency (if genuine).

### 3. Lead-Gen / Squeeze Page
Best for: single conversion action with minimal commitment (email opt-in, webinar registration, free download).
Sections: Hero (single benefit-driven headline) → 3-5 bullet benefits of the free offer → Single opt-in form/CTA → Minimal or no FAQ → Trust indicator (privacy note, subscriber count).

## Professional Guidance
Match page length to the size of the ask: the bigger the commitment (money or time), the more proof and objection-handling the page needs before the CTA will convert. A $20/month SaaS trial needs far less persuasion than a $2,000 course.

## Examples
A free ebook opt-in page converts best short and focused (single benefit + form); a $3,000 coaching program page typically needs the full long-form structure with multiple testimonials and a detailed FAQ to overcome price objections.

## Common Mistakes
- Using a long-form sales page structure for a low-commitment free offer (over-persuading, causing drop-off from fatigue)
- Using a short squeeze-page structure for a high-price offer (under-persuading, leaving objections unaddressed)
- Placing the CTA only once at the very bottom of a long page

## Best Practices
- Repeat the primary CTA at natural pause points throughout the page, not only at the very end.
- For long-form pages, use a sticky/anchor CTA concept (noted as a recommendation, since actual implementation is a dev task) so the CTA is always reachable.
- Keep squeeze pages to a single focused benefit — resist the urge to add more content.

## Summary
Choose short-form, long-form, or squeeze-page architecture based on the size of the commitment being asked of the visitor, and repeat the primary CTA at multiple natural points throughout.
