# Messaging Framework

## Purpose

This document explains how to identify and apply a brand's messaging pillars — the recurring substantive themes and value propositions a brand returns to — so that voice-consistent copy is also message-consistent, not just tonally similar.

## Introduction

Voice governs *how* something is said; messaging governs *what* is said. Two pieces of copy can be perfectly on-tone and still be off-message if they emphasize the wrong value proposition, contradict positioning, or omit a pillar the brand always leads with. Messaging consistency is what makes a brand's content feel strategically coherent over time, not just stylistically similar.

## Detailed Explanation

### Components of a Messaging Framework

1. **Positioning statement** — the one- or two-sentence core claim of what the brand is and who it's for (e.g., "the project management tool built for teams who hate project management tools").
2. **Messaging pillars** — 3-5 recurring themes/value props the brand returns to across content (e.g., speed, simplicity, human support, transparency). Every substantial piece of content should touch at least one pillar; a piece that ignores all of them is likely off-strategy even if well-written.
3. **Proof points** — the specific facts, features, or evidence that support each pillar (a pillar without proof reads as empty marketing-speak).
4. **Differentiators** — what the brand explicitly says or implies it is not, or how it differs from alternatives (used carefully — avoid disparaging real named competitors).
5. **Audience framing** — how the brand describes its reader/customer (e.g., "builders," "busy parents," "growth-stage teams") — this shapes word choice and examples used.

### Applying the Framework

When rewriting or generating content:
- Identify which pillar(s) the piece should carry, based on its purpose (a support article may need zero persuasive pillars; a product page needs several).
- Check that any claims made are backed by a proof point present in source material — do not invent proof points.
- Make sure the audience framing is used consistently (don't call the same audience "users" in one place and "customers" in another if the brand has a stated preference).

## Professional Guidance

- Messaging pillars should feel like recurring convictions, not a checklist to tick off in every sentence — overuse makes copy feel mechanical.
- When a brand's messaging framework isn't provided, infer likely pillars cautiously from example copy and flag them back to the user for confirmation rather than presenting inferred pillars as fact.
- Avoid manufacturing specific statistics, awards, or claims not present in the source — messaging strength comes from clear, honest emphasis, not invented proof points.

## Examples

**Pillar:** "Radically simple setup"
**Proof point (from source material):** "Most customers are live within 10 minutes."
**On-message sentence:** "No onboarding call, no manual — most teams are up and running in about ten minutes."
**Off-message sentence (generic, no pillar):** "Our platform offers a comprehensive suite of configurable features for modern teams."

## Common Mistakes

- Writing tonally perfect copy that emphasizes the wrong value proposition for the context (e.g., leading with "enterprise-grade security" in a piece meant to emphasize ease of use).
- Inventing proof points, statistics, or claims not present in source material to make messaging land harder.
- Inconsistent audience framing across pieces in the same batch.
- Overloading a single piece with every pillar at once instead of choosing the 1-2 most relevant.

## Best Practices

- Match pillar emphasis to the specific piece's purpose and channel.
- Always ground claims in the proof points actually supplied.
- When formalizing a messaging framework from scratch, derive it from repeated themes across multiple provided examples rather than a single piece.

## Summary

Messaging is the substantive counterpart to voice. Identify positioning, pillars, proof points, differentiators, and audience framing, apply the pillars most relevant to each piece's purpose, and never invent supporting facts that weren't in the source material.
