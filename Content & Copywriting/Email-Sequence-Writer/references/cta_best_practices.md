# CTA Best Practices (Email Context)

## Purpose
Guidance specific to writing calls to action inside email sequences, where CTA design differs from landing pages or ads.

## Introduction
An email CTA's job is to get one click toward one next step. Because inbox attention is fragmented, email CTAs must be simpler and lower-friction than a landing page CTA.

## Detailed Explanation
- **One CTA per email.** Multiple links to different destinations split attention and measurably reduce click-through on the one that matters.
- **Button + text link redundancy** is fine (same destination, two placements) but should not count as two different CTAs.
- **Match CTA commitment level to sequence position**: early sequence CTAs should be low-commitment (read more, watch this), later ones can ask for the sale/signup/call.
- **Use first-person or specific phrasing**: "Get my template" outperforms generic "Click here" or "Submit."
- **Place the primary CTA where it's reachable** without requiring a long scroll on mobile; repeat it as a text link in a P.S. for skimmers.

## Examples
- Weak: "Click here" / "Submit" / "Learn more" (generic, no ownership)
- Strong: "Send me the checklist" / "Save my seat" / "Show me the demo"

## Common Mistakes
- Including a "P.S." with a second, different offer than the main CTA
- Asking for high-commitment actions (purchase, demo call) in email 1 of a cold sequence
- Vague CTA text that doesn't state what happens after the click

## Best Practices
- Write the CTA before writing the surrounding body copy, so the email can be built to support that one action.
- Test first-person CTA phrasing ("Get my guide") against second-person ("Get your guide") if volume allows.

## Summary
One CTA, matched to the recipient's current relationship stage, phrased with specific ownership language.
