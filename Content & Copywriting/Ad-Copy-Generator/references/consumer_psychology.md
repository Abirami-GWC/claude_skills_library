# Consumer & Conversion Psychology

## Purpose

Explains the emotional and cognitive drivers behind effective ad copy, and draws a clear line between legitimate persuasion and manipulative dark patterns that this skill will not produce.

## Introduction

Effective ad copy taps into real psychological drivers — attention, desire, trust, urgency — but there's an important ethical line between using these honestly and exploiting them manipulatively. This reference equips Claude to do the former and refuse the latter.

## Detailed Explanation

### Core Emotional/Cognitive Drivers

1. **Pain avoidance** — people are often more motivated to avoid a loss/problem than to gain a benefit. Naming a specific pain point ("still doing X the hard way?") is often stronger than an abstract benefit claim.
2. **Aspiration/identity** — copy that reflects who the reader wants to be or be seen as ("built for people who take X seriously").
3. **Social proof** — humans look to others' behavior as a trust signal ("join X people who..."). Must use real, user-supplied figures only.
4. **Authority/credibility** — expert endorsement, certifications, data — again, only real, user-supplied claims.
5. **Scarcity/urgency** — perceived limited availability or time increases motivation to act — legitimate only when genuinely true.
6. **Reciprocity** — offering something of value up front (free tool, guide, trial) increases willingness to engage.
7. **Simplicity/ease** — reducing perceived effort ("takes 2 minutes," "no credit card required") lowers the psychological barrier to action.

### The Line Between Persuasion and Manipulation

| Legitimate persuasion | Manipulative dark pattern (avoid) |
|---|---|
| Real urgency ("sale ends Friday," confirmed by user) | Fabricated countdown timers/fake low-stock claims |
| Real social proof (actual user-supplied numbers) | Invented testimonials or user counts |
| Clear, honest value proposition | Curiosity hooks that don't pay off, bait-and-switch offers |
| Addressing a real pain point | Exploiting fear, insecurity, or vulnerability disproportionately, especially targeting vulnerable groups |
| Transparent pricing/offer terms | Hidden subscription terms, disguised recurring billing framed as one-time |
| Emphasizing genuine benefits | Disparaging specific named real competitors with unverifiable claims |

## Professional Guidance

- When a user's request implies fabricated urgency, invented social proof, or exploiting insecurity/fear disproportionately (e.g., body-image-based fear appeals to a vulnerable audience), decline that specific element and offer an honest alternative that still converts.
- Emotional appeals should be proportionate — using fear/pain framing for a genuinely urgent problem (e.g., a security vulnerability) is reasonable; using it to manufacture anxiety about a minor cosmetic concern targeted at insecure audiences is not something to lean into.
- Reciprocity and ease-of-action framing are almost always safe, honest levers to reach for first.

## Examples

**Legitimate pain-avoidance hook:** "Missed invoices cost freelancers an average of $6,000/year in unpaid work — here's how to stop that." (if the $6,000 figure is user-supplied and real)
**Manipulative version (avoid):** Inventing a scary, unverified statistic to manufacture anxiety not grounded in the user's actual data.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating "persuasive" as synonymous with "exaggerated" or "manipulative."
- Using fear appeals disproportionate to the actual stakes of the product/offer.
- Inventing social proof numbers because the user didn't supply real ones.
- Applying fake urgency by default rather than asking whether real urgency exists.

## Best Practices

- Ask whether urgency/scarcity claims are real before using them; if not confirmed, omit them rather than inventing them.
- Match emotional intensity to the actual stakes of the offer.
- Prefer specific, verifiable claims over vague emotionally-loaded superlatives.

## Summary

Effective ad copy draws on real psychological drivers — pain avoidance, aspiration, social proof, authority, scarcity, reciprocity, ease — but only when grounded in truth. Fabricated urgency, invented social proof, and disproportionate exploitation of fear or insecurity are dark patterns this skill declines to produce, offering honest, still-effective alternatives instead.
