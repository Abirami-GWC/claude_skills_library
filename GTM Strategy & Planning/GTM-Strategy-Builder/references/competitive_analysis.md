# Competitive Analysis

**Purpose of this document:** Give Claude a rigorous, standard method for analyzing competitive dynamics and translating that analysis into GTM-relevant differentiation, independent of industry.

## Introduction

Competitive analysis in a GTM context is not an academic exercise — its purpose is to sharpen positioning (see `positioning_framework.md`), inform messaging proof points (see `messaging_framework.md`), and guide sales enablement. This document covers industry-structure analysis (why the competitive landscape behaves as it does) and buyer-level competitive analysis (why specific deals are won or lost), which together should ground any competitive positioning claim.

## Detailed Explanation

### Industry Structure Analysis (Five Forces)
A widely used framework for understanding the intensity and nature of competition in a market examines five forces:
1. **Rivalry among existing competitors** — how many players, how similar their offerings, how price-sensitive the market is.
2. **Threat of new entrants** — how easy it is for new players to enter (barriers to entry: capital, technology, brand, regulation).
3. **Bargaining power of buyers** — how much leverage customers have (concentration, switching cost, availability of alternatives).
4. **Bargaining power of suppliers** — how dependent the company is on suppliers/partners/platforms it relies on.
5. **Threat of substitutes** — non-obvious alternatives that solve the same underlying job (including manual processes, adjacent categories, or "do nothing").
This structural view helps explain *why* a market behaves as it does (e.g., high rivalry and low switching cost imply differentiation and retention must be prioritized over pure acquisition) and should inform GTM motion and messaging emphasis, not just be treated as a one-time academic exercise.

### Competitive Alternative Mapping
For GTM purposes, map the full set of competitive alternatives a target buyer actually considers, in three tiers:
- **Direct competitors** — same category, similar solution approach.
- **Indirect/adjacent competitors** — different approach, same underlying job solved.
- **Status quo/non-consumption** — manual process, spreadsheet, in-house build, or simply not solving the problem at all. This is frequently the most common "competitor" and is often under-analyzed.

### Win/Loss Analysis
The most reliable source of real competitive insight is structured win/loss analysis: interviewing (or reviewing notes from) prospects after they decide, regardless of outcome, to learn:
- What alternatives were seriously considered.
- What criteria actually drove the decision (as opposed to the criteria assumed internally).
- Where messaging/positioning landed or fell flat during the evaluation.
- What almost caused a loss even in a won deal, and what almost caused a win even in a lost deal.
Win/loss data should directly update positioning differentiators and messaging proof points — it is the empirical check on whether the company's assumed differentiation is the differentiation buyers actually perceive and value.

### Battlecards
A battlecard is a tactical sales-enablement artifact (distinct from the strategic outputs of this skill) that translates competitive analysis into: how to respond to specific competitor mentions, trap-setting questions to ask prospects, and objection-handling scripts. This skill informs the strategic competitive positioning that a battlecard would operationalize, but does not itself produce a full tactical battlecard document — that is a distinct, more sales-tactical artifact.

### Differentiation Levels
Competitive differentiation can occur at multiple levels, and a strong strategy is explicit about which level(s) it's claiming:
- **Capability-level** — a specific feature/technical capability competitors lack.
- **Outcome-level** — a business result achieved better/faster/cheaper, regardless of how.
- **Experience-level** — ease of adoption, support quality, time-to-value.
- **Business-model-level** — pricing structure, packaging, or commercial terms that change the buying calculus itself.
The strongest positioning typically combines a capability-level proof point with an outcome-level claim, since outcome claims alone are easy to assert and hard to substantiate without a concrete capability behind them.

## Professional Guidance

Never assert a competitive differentiator without evidence — ground claims in win/loss data, documented capability gaps, or credible, cited third-party comparisons where possible. Always include the status quo/non-consumption alternative in competitive mapping, since it is frequently the largest real competitor and the most commonly overlooked. Recommend a lightweight, recurring win/loss review cadence (even quarterly, with a handful of interviews) rather than treating competitive analysis as a one-time exercise.

## Examples

- *B2B SaaS:* Direct competitor = another workflow automation vendor; indirect = a broader project-management suite with adjacent automation features; status quo = manual spreadsheet-based tracking, which analysis shows is the actual alternative in the majority of losses.
- *Healthcare:* Direct competitor = another compliance documentation platform; status quo = paper-based or generic document-management systems repurposed for compliance, revealed via win/loss interviews to be the primary competitive alternative in smaller accounts.
- *Industrial:* Direct competitor = another condition-monitoring sensor vendor; indirect = manual inspection rounds by maintenance staff, still the dominant approach in the segment and the most common "alternative" cited in lost deals.

## Common Mistakes

- Analyzing only direct, named competitors and ignoring status quo/non-consumption, which is often the real primary alternative.
- Asserting differentiation based on internal belief rather than win/loss evidence or documented capability comparison.
- Treating competitive analysis as a one-time exercise instead of a recurring, lightweight cadence tied to actual deal outcomes.
- Conflating strategic competitive positioning with tactical sales battlecard content, producing a document that is neither strategically grounded nor tactically usable.
- Making outcome-level claims ("saves you X% cost") without a capability-level mechanism substantiating how that outcome is produced.

## Best Practices

- Map competitors in three tiers: direct, indirect/adjacent, and status quo/non-consumption.
- Use the five-forces lens periodically to check whether GTM emphasis (acquisition vs. retention, differentiation vs. price) still matches the market's actual structural dynamics.
- Run structured win/loss interviews on a recurring cadence and feed findings directly back into positioning and messaging updates.
- Pair every outcome-level differentiation claim with the capability-level mechanism that produces it.
- Keep strategic competitive analysis (this skill) and tactical battlecard production (a downstream sales-enablement artifact) clearly distinct.

## Summary

Competitive analysis for GTM purposes combines industry-structure analysis (five forces) with buyer-level competitive alternative mapping (direct, indirect, and status quo) and empirical win/loss analysis. Findings should directly and continuously update positioning differentiators and messaging proof points, with differentiation claims grounded in evidence and expressed at the appropriate level (capability, outcome, experience, or business-model). This strategic analysis informs, but is distinct from, tactical sales battlecards.
