# Messaging Framework

**Purpose of this document:** Give Claude a standard method for translating positioning and persona insight into a structured, consistent messaging framework, independent of industry.

## Introduction

Messaging is the set of specific words and claims used to communicate value to a target audience across channels — the customer-facing expression of positioning (see `positioning_framework.md`) tailored to specific personas (see `buyer_persona_guide.md`). Without a shared messaging framework, different teams (sales, marketing, product, support) tend to describe the same offering inconsistently, diluting brand clarity and confusing prospects who encounter multiple touchpoints.

## Detailed Explanation

### The Messaging House / Pillar Structure
A messaging framework is commonly structured as a "house":
- **Foundation:** positioning statement and core value proposition (the roof/foundation that everything else rests on).
- **Pillars (typically 3-4):** the primary differentiated value themes, each independently defensible and each mapped to specific proof points and personas.
- **Proof points:** the evidence for each pillar (capability specifics, methodology, outcome data, third-party validation) — claims without proof points are unpersuasive assertions.
- **Persona-specific variants:** how each pillar is phrased and prioritized differently for each persona/segment (the technical evaluator needs a different articulation of the same pillar than the economic buyer).

Limiting to 3-4 pillars is deliberate: more than that dilutes focus and is difficult for sales teams to retain and use consistently in conversation.

### Value Proposition Canvas Logic
A widely used method for grounding messaging in real customer need pairs two sides:
- **Customer profile side:** the persona's jobs (functional/emotional/social, see `buyer_persona_guide.md`), pains (obstacles, risks, frustrations with the status quo), and gains (desired outcomes, "nice to haves").
- **Value map side:** the product's pain relievers (how it specifically addresses each named pain) and gain creators (how it specifically produces each named desired outcome), plus the core offering itself.
Strong messaging exists only where a specific pain reliever or gain creator maps directly to a specific, named customer pain or gain — generic value statements not traceable to a specific pain/gain are weak messaging and should be revised or cut.

### Narrative / StoryBrand-Style Structure
For narrative-driven messaging (website copy, sales narratives, campaign copy), a widely used story structure positions the customer — not the company — as the hero:
1. A character (the customer) has a problem.
2. They meet a guide (the company/product) that has empathy and authority (proof points).
3. The guide gives them a plan (simple, clear steps to engage).
4. The plan calls them to action.
5. The narrative shows what success looks like (and, optionally, what failure/inaction looks like).
This structure is most useful for top-of-funnel and mid-funnel narrative content; it complements, rather than replaces, the pillar/proof-point structure used for detailed sales and product messaging.

### Message Testing and Hierarchy
Not all messages carry equal weight in every context. A messaging framework should define a hierarchy: the single most important message (used first, in headlines, in the first 10 seconds of a sales call) versus supporting messages (used in subsequent detail, FAQs, deeper content). Message hierarchy should shift by persona and by funnel stage — the message that captures attention at awareness stage is often not the message that closes a decision at evaluation stage.

## Professional Guidance

Always derive messaging pillars from the positioning's key differentiator(s) and from persona pains/gains — never draft pillars as a brainstorm disconnected from those inputs. Each pillar must have at least one proof point; if no proof point exists yet, flag the pillar as aspirational/needs-validation rather than presenting it as market-ready. Ensure each persona has an explicit variant of the core message, not just the general pillars restated.

## Examples

- *Pillar with proof point (B2B SaaS):* Pillar — "Get from signup to first insight in under a day." Proof point — "median customer completes first integration in 6 hours, based on onboarding telemetry." Persona variant for technical evaluator — emphasize the integration architecture and security review artifacts available; persona variant for economic buyer — emphasize time-to-value and reduced implementation cost.
- *Pillar with proof point (healthcare):* Pillar — "Always audit-ready, not scrambling before inspection." Proof point — "continuous evidence collection eliminates the multi-week manual audit-prep cycle." Persona variant for compliance director — emphasize audit-trail completeness; persona variant for clinical staff user — emphasize minimal workflow disruption.
- *Pillar with proof point (industrial):* Pillar — "Catch failures before they become downtime." Proof point — "sensor-based early warning detects the failure signatures that precede unplanned stoppages, validated across pilot deployments." Persona variant for reliability engineer — emphasize alert accuracy and false-positive rate; persona variant for plant financial owner — emphasize avoided downtime cost.

## Common Mistakes

- Writing messaging pillars as generic superlatives ("best-in-class," "easy to use") without a specific proof point.
- Using the exact same message for every persona instead of creating persona-specific variants of shared pillars.
- Having more than 4-5 pillars, which sales teams cannot retain or use consistently.
- Building narrative/story-style content without first establishing the underlying pillar/proof-point structure it should be consistent with.
- Letting messaging drift from positioning over time as different teams write copy independently without a shared reference.

## Best Practices

- Derive every pillar directly from positioning's differentiator and from persona pain/gain research.
- Require a proof point for every pillar; explicitly flag any pillar lacking one as unvalidated.
- Cap pillars at 3-4 and create named persona-specific variants rather than separate unrelated messages.
- Define a message hierarchy (primary vs. supporting) that can shift by funnel stage and persona.
- Maintain the messaging framework as the single source of truth that all customer-facing teams reference, updating it centrally rather than allowing per-team drift.

## Summary

Messaging translates positioning and persona research into a structured "house" of 3-4 differentiated pillars, each backed by a specific proof point and expressed with persona-specific variants. The value proposition canvas (pains/gains mapped to pain relievers/gain creators) ensures messaging is traceable to real customer need rather than generic claims, while narrative structures (customer-as-hero) are useful for top-of-funnel content built on top of that same pillar foundation. A shared, centrally maintained messaging framework prevents inconsistent external communication across sales, marketing, and product.
