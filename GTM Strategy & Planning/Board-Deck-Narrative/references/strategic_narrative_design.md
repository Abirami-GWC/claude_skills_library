# Strategic Narrative Design

**Purpose of this file:** Explain how to shape a strategic narrative arc — context, conflict/tension, resolution — and apply "so what" framing so that a board narrative reads as a coherent story rather than a list of facts.

## Introduction

Boards and executives do not remember lists of facts; they remember stories with a clear shape. Strategic narrative design borrows from classic storytelling structure — establishing context, surfacing the central tension or conflict, and presenting a resolution — to make a business update memorable, persuasive, and easy to follow. This reference explains how to build that arc for different board situations (steady progress, course correction, underperformance, pivot, or investment ask) and how to apply "so what" framing throughout so every fact connects to its business implication.

## Detailed Explanation

### The Context-Conflict-Resolution Arc

Every strong strategic narrative has three movements:

1. **Context** — a brief, plain-language statement of the situation: where the business or initiative stood at the start of the period, and what conditions (market, competitive, internal) shaped it. This should be short — a few sentences, not a page — and should avoid restating things the board already knows in exhaustive detail.
2. **Conflict / Tension** — the central challenge, obstacle, or decision point the business faced. This is the heart of the narrative: what changed, what went wrong or right, what tradeoff had to be made, or what uncertainty remains. Naming the tension honestly is what makes the narrative credible; a narrative with no tension reads as either trivial or evasive.
3. **Resolution** — how the tension was (or will be) addressed: the action taken, the result achieved, or the recommendation being made to resolve what remains open. This is where the ask or forward plan lives.

This arc applies whether the news is good (steady progress narrative: context of the plan, tension of a real obstacle overcome, resolution of results achieved) or difficult (underperformance narrative: context of the target, tension of the miss and its cause, resolution of the corrective plan).

### Matching the Arc to the Situation

- **Steady progress:** Context = the plan; Conflict = the specific challenge that had to be overcome to stay on track; Resolution = current results and what continued execution looks like. Avoid a narrative with no conflict at all — "everything went as planned" reads as thin even when true; name the real challenge that was managed.
- **Course correction:** Context = original plan; Conflict = the signal that the original approach was not working; Resolution = the adjustment made and early evidence it is working.
- **Underperformance:** Context = the target; Conflict = the miss and its root cause, stated plainly; Resolution = the corrective plan and expected timeline to recovery. Do not skip or soften the conflict here — boards read a narrative that glosses over a miss as less trustworthy, not more reassuring.
- **Pivot:** Context = the prior strategy and why it made sense at the time; Conflict = the evidence that conditions have changed or that the prior approach has a ceiling; Resolution = the new direction, why it addresses the tension, and what is being asked of the board (approval, resources, patience).
- **Investment ask:** Context = current state and plan; Conflict = the constraint or opportunity that requires additional investment to address; Resolution = the specific ask, expected return, and alternative considered.

### "So What" Framing

Every fact, metric, or event included in the narrative should be followed, implicitly or explicitly, by its business implication — the "so what." A fact without a "so what" is trivia; a fact with a "so what" is information a board can act on.

- Fact: "Conversion rate declined two points this quarter."
- So what: "...which puts full-year pipeline coverage at risk unless addressed within the next six weeks, which is why we are proposing [action]."

Applying "so what" discipline consistently is often the single highest-leverage edit to a draft narrative: for every sentence, ask "does the reader now know why this matters," and if not, add or surface the implication.

## Professional Guidance

- Identify the arc (steady progress, course correction, underperformance, pivot, investment ask) before drafting; it determines what belongs in each section and in what order.
- Name the central tension honestly and early — do not let it emerge only in Q&A.
- Keep the context section brief; boards do not need a full history restated each cycle.
- End the resolution section with a clear statement of what, if anything, is being asked of the board.
- Apply "so what" framing to every metric or fact included — if it has no implication, consider cutting it.

## Examples

**Underperformance narrative (well-shaped):** "We set a target of [X] for the quarter (context). We missed that target by [Y], driven primarily by [specific cause] (conflict, stated plainly). We have already made [specific change] and expect results to recover by [timeframe] (resolution)."

**Pivot narrative (well-shaped):** "We entered the year pursuing [prior approach], which made sense given [prior conditions] (context). Over the last two quarters, [specific evidence] showed this approach has a ceiling we will not overcome without disproportionate investment (conflict). We are recommending a shift to [new direction], which addresses that ceiling directly, and are asking the board to approve [specific ask] to support the transition (resolution)."

**Fact without "so what" (avoid):** "We published fourteen pieces of content this quarter."

**Fact with "so what":** "Content-driven pipeline grew [X]% this quarter, becoming our fastest-growing source of qualified pipeline — which is why we are proposing to reallocate additional budget toward it."

## Common Mistakes

- Presenting a narrative with no real tension, which reads as either thin or evasive.
- Burying the central conflict deep in the material instead of naming it early.
- Restating extensive historical context the board already knows, crowding out the resolution.
- Including facts or metrics with no stated business implication.
- Ending without a clear resolution or ask, leaving the board unsure what happens next.

## Best Practices

- Choose the arc type deliberately based on the situation before drafting.
- Keep context brief, name tension honestly, and make the resolution (including any ask) explicit.
- Apply "so what" framing to every fact or metric in the narrative.
- Maintain narrative honesty even under compression — a good story is not the same as an overly positive one.
- Reuse arc structure across cycles so the board recognizes the shape of the update even as content changes.

## Summary

A strategic narrative becomes memorable and persuasive when it is shaped as context, conflict, and resolution rather than presented as an unordered list of facts, and when every fact is paired with its "so what" implication. Selecting the right arc for the situation — steady progress, course correction, underperformance, pivot, or investment ask — and applying these disciplines consistently is central to producing a board narrative that reads as a coherent, credible story.
