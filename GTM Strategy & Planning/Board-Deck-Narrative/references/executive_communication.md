# Executive Communication Principles

**Purpose of this file:** Provide the core communication frameworks — pyramid principle, BLUF, and audience-altitude calibration — that this skill uses whenever it drafts any board or executive artifact.

## Introduction

Executive and board audiences consume information differently than operational teams. They have limited time, sit one or more levels removed from day-to-day execution, and are typically making a small number of consequential decisions rather than tracking a large number of tasks. Communicating effectively to this audience is a distinct discipline, not just "the same content, shorter." This reference covers the three foundational principles this skill relies on: the pyramid principle, bottom-line-up-front (BLUF) framing, and audience-tailored altitude.

## Detailed Explanation

### The Pyramid Principle

The pyramid principle, originally developed for management consulting writing, structures communication top-down: state the conclusion first, then the key supporting arguments (typically three to five), then the detailed evidence beneath each argument. This inverts how most people think and write — most first-drafts build up to a conclusion; the pyramid principle asks the writer to state the conclusion, then work backward to justify it. For board materials, this means the first sentence or slide should contain the answer to "what do you want us to know or decide," not scene-setting context.

A pyramid-structured document has three layers:
1. **Governing thought** — the single takeaway or recommendation.
2. **Key line** — the three to five reasons or themes supporting the governing thought.
3. **Supporting detail** — data, examples, and sub-arguments beneath each key line item, available but not front-loaded.

### BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)

BLUF is a related, simpler discipline: whatever the reader needs to act on or remember must appear in the first sentence or paragraph, not the last. For board documents, BLUF typically takes the form of a single sentence naming the situation, the ask (if any), and the recommended path, e.g., "We are on track to exceed the annual retention target and are requesting board approval to reallocate [X]% of the underspent budget toward expansion capacity." Everything else in the document supports, contextualizes, or defends that sentence.

### Audience-Tailored Altitude

"Altitude" refers to the level of abstraction appropriate to the audience. A frontline team needs task-level detail; a functional leader needs program-level detail; a board needs strategic and financial implications only. A common failure mode is writing board materials at the same altitude used for internal team updates — listing initiatives, tools, or tactics rather than outcomes, risks, and decisions. The test for correct altitude is: could the same sentence be said, unchanged, to an external stakeholder unfamiliar with internal systems or teams? If a sentence relies on internal shorthand, tool names, or team names to be understood, it is likely pitched too low for a board audience.

## Professional Guidance

- Draft the BLUF sentence last, after the analysis is complete, but place it first in the document.
- When compressing a long analysis, ask "if the reader only reads this paragraph, do they still get the decision right?"
- Reserve appendices or backup slides for detail that supports but is not required for the top-line conclusion.
- For recurring board relationships, calibrate altitude to what that specific board has previously asked for — some boards want more operational detail than others, but the default should always be higher altitude, with detail available on request.
- Every major claim at the top of the pyramid should be traceable to a specific piece of evidence lower in the structure, even if that evidence is not shown on the main slide.

## Examples

**Poor altitude (too low):** "The lifecycle marketing team completed migration of the nurture workflow to the new automation platform and is now running four active campaign sequences targeting mid-funnel accounts."

**Correct altitude:** "Investment in mid-funnel nurture has begun converting stalled pipeline; we expect this to contribute [X]% of net-new pipeline by next quarter."

**Poor BLUF (buried conclusion):** A three-paragraph document that describes market conditions, then team activities, then finally states in the last paragraph, "As a result, we recommend delaying the launch by one quarter."

**Correct BLUF:** "We recommend delaying the launch by one quarter. Market conditions and two unresolved dependencies below explain why."

## Common Mistakes

- Leading with context or history instead of the conclusion, forcing the reader to wait for the point.
- Including internal team, tool, or system names that require translation for an outside audience.
- Presenting more than five key supporting arguments, diluting the pyramid's clarity.
- Mistaking "shorter" for "higher altitude" — a short document can still be pitched at the wrong level if it lists tactics instead of outcomes.
- Failing to make the ask explicit when one exists, leaving the board to infer what they are being asked to do.

## Best Practices

- Write the BLUF sentence as a standalone test: if it were the only sentence read, would the reader know the situation and the decision needed?
- Limit the key line to three to five points; if there are more, group them into higher-order themes.
| Group tactical detail under strategic themes rather than listing every initiative flat.
- Use consistent altitude throughout a single document — do not drop into task-level detail in the middle of an otherwise strategic narrative.
- When in doubt about altitude, err toward removing detail rather than adding it; detail can be provided verbally if asked.

## Summary

Effective executive communication rests on three disciplines: structuring arguments top-down via the pyramid principle, leading with the bottom line so the reader never has to search for the point, and calibrating altitude so content reflects strategic implications rather than operational activity. Applying these disciplines consistently is what separates a board-ready narrative from an internal status update that has simply been shortened.
