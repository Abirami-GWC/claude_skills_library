# Board Reporting Norms

**Purpose of this file:** Explain what boards typically expect from management reporting — content, cadence, and governance context — so outputs from this skill match the norms of a board setting rather than an internal business review.

## Introduction

A board is a governance body, not an operating team. Its members are typically responsible for oversight, fiduciary duty, and a small number of high-stakes approvals (budget, strategy changes, major hires, risk posture) rather than for running the business day to day. Reporting to a board therefore has different norms than an internal quarterly business review: less operational detail, more emphasis on trend and trajectory, explicit framing of what is being asked of the board versus what is being shared for information only, and consistent structure across meetings so members can track change over time.

## Detailed Explanation

### What Boards Expect

- **Trajectory over snapshot.** Boards want to know whether things are getting better or worse relative to plan, not just the current-period number in isolation.
- **Consistency across cycles.** The same core metrics and structure should appear meeting over meeting so members can build a mental model over time. Changing the reporting format every cycle erodes trust and slows comprehension.
- **Clear separation of "for information" vs. "for decision."** Boards typically distinguish between material shared to keep them informed and material presented because a vote or approval is being requested. Conflating the two causes confusion about what is actually being asked.
- **Honest treatment of bad news.** Boards generally expect underperformance to be named plainly, with cause and forward plan, rather than buried in a "context" section or diluted by unrelated good news.
- **Governance-appropriate framing of risk.** Boards are often legally and fiduciarily responsible for risk oversight; risks should be named in terms of business and financial exposure, not internal technical or process detail.

### Cadence and Its Effect on Content

Reporting cadence (monthly, quarterly, or ad hoc) shapes what belongs in a given cycle:
- **Regular cadence cycles** (e.g., quarterly) typically carry a standing structure: performance against plan, key metric trends, notable risks, and any decisions needed that cycle.
- **Ad hoc or special sessions** (e.g., a pivot approval, an emergency budget request) typically narrow to the single decision at hand, with only the context necessary to support that decision — not a full business review.
- Boards build expectations over time about what "normal" reporting looks like; deviating from that norm (e.g., suddenly presenting far more or far less detail) itself signals something to the board, so any change in structure should be intentional and flagged.

### Governance Context

Board members often carry fiduciary or oversight obligations that shape how they read management materials. This means:
- Material presented as a formal ask (e.g., budget approval) should be clearly distinguishable from material presented as an update, since the board's formal response (a vote, a minute, a resolution) may depend on this distinction.
- Historical decisions and prior commitments referenced by the board should be acknowledged when relevant (e.g., "as approved last quarter," "consistent with the strategy approved in Q1") rather than presented as if for the first time.
- Confidentiality and distribution matter — board materials often circulate beyond the room (to auditors, counsel, or a wider set of stakeholders), so sensitive figures should be framed at a level of precision appropriate to that broader distribution unless the audience is confirmed to be restricted.

## Professional Guidance

- Open every board cycle's material with a short "since last time" framing that connects to the prior cycle's discussion or decisions.
- Explicitly label sections as "For Decision," "For Discussion," or "For Information" so the board immediately knows how to engage with each part.
- When presenting a metric, always pair it with the trend and, where available, the plan or benchmark — a single point-in-time number without context invites the wrong question.
- When reporting bad news, pair it with: what happened, why, what is being done, and by when results should change — in that order.
- Keep the standing structure stable across cycles; introduce new sections only when there is a durable reason to keep tracking that topic going forward.

## Examples

**Poorly framed board update:** A dense narrative mixing routine metrics, a budget approval request, and a minor process change into one undifferentiated section, leaving the board unsure what requires a vote.

**Well-framed board update:** Three clearly labeled sections — "For Information: Q2 performance against plan," "For Decision: approval of a 15% budget increase for the next two quarters," and "For Discussion: emerging risk in a key channel" — each with its own brief rationale.

**Poorly handled bad news:** A single line buried mid-deck noting a miss on a key target with no explanation or forward plan.

**Well-handled bad news:** A dedicated section stating the miss, the primary driver, the corrective action already underway, and the expected quarter of recovery.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating a board deck like an internal all-hands update, with excessive operational detail and internal terminology.
- Failing to distinguish clearly between what needs a vote and what is shared for awareness.
- Changing the reporting structure every cycle, making trend-tracking difficult for members.
- Softening or omitting bad news, which damages credibility more than the bad news itself.
- Presenting risk in technical or process terms rather than business and financial impact terms.

## Best Practices

- Maintain a standing template across board cycles and only evolve it deliberately.
- Always state clearly whether a section is informational or requires a decision.
- Reference prior board decisions and commitments explicitly when relevant to current material.
- Frame all risk and bad news in terms of business impact, cause, and corrective action.
- Assume broader distribution of board materials than just the room, and calibrate sensitive detail accordingly.

## Summary

Board reporting differs from internal reporting in its emphasis on trajectory, consistency, explicit decision framing, and governance-appropriate treatment of risk and bad news. Understanding cadence and governance context ensures the narrative this skill produces matches what a board actually expects to receive, rather than simply being a shorter version of an internal update.
