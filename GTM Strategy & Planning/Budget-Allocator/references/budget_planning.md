# Budget Planning Frameworks

## Purpose
This document provides standalone reference knowledge on GTM budget planning methodologies — top-down, bottom-up, and zero-based approaches — so that a budget allocation exercise can be grounded in a defensible, repeatable framework rather than ad-hoc judgment.

## Introduction
Budget planning is the process of deciding, in advance of spending, how much money will be committed to which GTM activities over a defined period (monthly, quarterly, annual). The choice of planning framework shapes what data is required, how fast the plan can be produced, and how defensible it is when challenged by finance or leadership. Three frameworks dominate GTM practice: top-down, bottom-up, and zero-based budgeting. Most mature organizations blend all three depending on the maturity of the channel and the stakes of the decision.

## Detailed Explanation

### Top-Down Budgeting
Leadership sets a total budget figure (often as a percentage of projected revenue, e.g., 10-20% of forecast revenue for GTM spend) and then allocates it downward to channels and teams based on prior-year splits, strategic priorities, or executive judgment.
- **Inputs needed**: total revenue target, target spend-to-revenue ratio, prior-year allocation percentages.
- **Speed**: fast — can be produced in hours with minimal data.
- **Risk**: may not reflect actual channel-level efficiency; can perpetuate historical bias (a channel keeps getting funded because it always has, not because it performs best).

### Bottom-Up Budgeting
Each channel or program owner builds a plan from granular assumptions (cost per lead, conversion rates, target pipeline, headcount costs) and the totals are summed to produce the overall budget, which is then reconciled against what the organization can afford.
- **Inputs needed**: channel-level unit economics (CAC, conversion rates by stage), target volume of leads/opportunities/customers, known fixed costs (tools, contracts, salaries).
- **Speed**: slower — requires more data collection and cross-functional input.
- **Risk**: if unit economics are outdated or channel owners are optimistic, the bottom-up total can overshoot what's actually affordable, requiring a reconciliation pass.

### Zero-Based Budgeting (ZBB)
Every dollar must be justified from zero each planning cycle — nothing is carried forward simply because it was funded last period. Each channel or program must present its expected return to compete for funding alongside every other option.
- **Inputs needed**: full unit economics and expected ROI/CAC/LTV for every channel and program, including ones not currently funded.
- **Speed**: slowest and most rigorous — typically used annually or when a significant strategy reset is underway (new leadership, budget cut, new market entry).
- **Risk**: highest analytical burden; can under-value brand/awareness activities whose payoff is long-cycle and hard to quantify in a single period.

### Choosing a Framework
- Use **top-down** when speed matters more than precision, when historical data is thin, or as a first-pass sanity check on a bottom-up build.
- Use **bottom-up** when channel-level historical data is reliable and the organization wants an efficiency-driven plan.
- Use **zero-based** when facing a budget cut, a strategy reset, entry into a new market/segment, or when leadership suspects entrenched, low-performing spend.
- In practice, run bottom-up for granularity, validate against top-down guardrails, and apply zero-based discipline to the lowest-performing 20-30% of current spend each cycle.

## Professional Guidance
- Always reconcile the bottom-up sum against the top-down ceiling before finalizing; the gap itself is a useful signal (a large gap suggests either the growth target or the budget ceiling needs revisiting).
- Document assumptions inline with every number — a budget model without visible assumptions cannot be defended or updated later.
- Revisit the plan at least once per quarter even in an annual planning cycle; treat the initial plan as a living model, not a one-time artifact.
- When data is missing for a channel, do not omit it from the model — include it with a clearly labeled benchmark estimate and a note on what data would replace the estimate.

## Examples
**Top-down example**: A company projects $10M in annual revenue and targets a 12% GTM spend ratio, yielding a $1.2M budget, split 40% paid, 25% events, 20% content/organic, 15% partner, matching last year's ratios pending further analysis.

**Bottom-up example**: Paid channel owner projects 500 leads/month at $150 CAC-per-lead-equivalent cost driving 25 customers/month at $3,000 fully-loaded CAC; multiplied across channels, the bottom-up sum totals $1.45M against a $1.2M ceiling, forcing a prioritization pass.

**Zero-based example**: Facing a 20% budget cut, the team scores all seven channels on ROAS and LTV:CAC, funds the top four fully, funds the fifth partially, and defunds the bottom two entirely rather than trimming every channel by an even 20%.

## Common Mistakes
- Allocating budget purely on last year's percentages without checking current channel performance (top-down without a bottom-up sanity check).
- Building a bottom-up plan with stale or aspirational conversion assumptions that inflate the case for a favored channel.
- Treating zero-based budgeting as a one-time event rather than a recurring discipline applied to the weakest-performing spend each cycle.
- Failing to reconcile bottom-up totals against the actual available budget, producing a plan that is immediately over budget.
- Omitting new or small channels from the model because they lack a full history, which biases the plan toward legacy channels.

## Best Practices
- Blend frameworks: top-down for the ceiling, bottom-up for the detail, zero-based discipline for the weakest performers.
- Express every allocation as both a dollar amount and a percentage of total budget for easy sensitivity checking.
- Build in a contingency/reserve line (typically 5-10% of total budget) for mid-cycle opportunities or corrections.
- Re-validate unit economics inputs at least quarterly; stale CAC/conversion assumptions are the most common source of bad plans.

## Summary
Top-down, bottom-up, and zero-based budgeting each answer a different question — how much can we spend, what does the detail justify, and does every dollar still earn its place. A production-grade GTM budget plan typically blends all three: a top-down ceiling for discipline, a bottom-up build for granularity, and zero-based scrutiny applied to the lowest-performing spend each cycle.
