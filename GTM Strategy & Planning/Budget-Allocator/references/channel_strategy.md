# Channel Strategy

## Purpose
This document provides standalone reference knowledge on GTM channel mix strategy — paid, organic, partner, events, and account-based marketing (ABM) — to guide how budget should be distributed across channel types based on their structural characteristics, not just their current-period metrics.

## Introduction
A channel mix decision is not only a question of "which channel has the best CAC/ROI right now" — it also depends on each channel type's structural properties: how quickly it scales, how long it takes to show results, how defensible its returns are against competitor bidding or algorithm changes, and how it complements other channels (e.g., brand/organic activity often improves paid channel efficiency by increasing search volume and ad relevance). A durable channel strategy balances near-term efficiency against portfolio resilience.

## Detailed Explanation

### Paid Channels (Search, Social, Display, Programmatic)
- **Characteristics**: fast to launch, fast to measure, highly scalable in the short term, but subject to auction dynamics (cost per click/impression rises as competitors bid up), and returns can evaporate quickly if spend stops (little residual effect).
- **When to prioritize**: when the business needs fast, measurable pipeline, when target audiences are well-defined and searchable/targetable, and when the team has the attribution infrastructure to measure return quickly.
- **Risk**: over-reliance creates a "pay-to-play" dependency with no compounding asset; rising CAC over time as auctions saturate.

### Organic / Content / SEO
- **Characteristics**: slow to build (often 6-18 months to show meaningful traffic/lead volume), but compounds over time and has a much lower marginal cost per lead once established; also improves paid channel efficiency indirectly.
- **When to prioritize**: for long-term CAC reduction, for categories with substantial organic search demand, and as a hedge against rising paid channel costs.
- **Risk**: hard to attribute precisely; can be underfunded because its payoff doesn't show up in short-cycle reporting.

### Partner / Channel / Referral
- **Characteristics**: leverages a third party's existing audience/trust; CAC is often lower per acquired customer but involves revenue share or partner enablement cost; scaling is gated by partner capacity and relationship quality, not just budget.
- **When to prioritize**: when trusted third-party relationships exist in the target market, or when direct-to-buyer trust/credibility is a barrier.
- **Risk**: less control over messaging and pipeline velocity; concentration risk if too dependent on a small number of partners.

### Events / Field Marketing
- **Characteristics**: high cost per touch but can be highly effective for relationship-building, longer sales cycles, and higher-value accounts; return is realized over a longer horizon (often 2+ quarters) and is harder to attribute cleanly.
- **When to prioritize**: for high-value, high-consideration purchases, competitive markets where in-person relationship-building differentiates, or category education needs.
- **Risk**: easy to overspend without disciplined post-event pipeline tracking; return can be overstated or understated depending on attribution window.

### Account-Based Marketing (ABM)
- **Characteristics**: concentrates spend on a defined list of high-value target accounts rather than broad-reach tactics; typically blends paid, content, and sales-development effort aimed at a named account list; efficient when average deal size is high and the number of viable target accounts is naturally limited.
- **When to prioritize**: when the addressable market is concentrated among a relatively small number of high-value accounts, or when expansion/cross-sell into named accounts is a priority.
- **Risk**: not efficient for markets with a large number of small-value buyers; requires tight sales-marketing alignment to execute well.

### Building a Channel Mix
There is no universal "correct" mix — the right blend depends on: average deal size, number of addressable accounts/buyers, sales cycle length, category search demand, and how much of the budget must produce results within the current period versus building durable, compounding advantage. A useful starting heuristic is to intentionally split budget across a "now" tier (fast-payback channels funding current-period pipeline), a "next" tier (channels with 2-4 quarter payback), and a "later" tier (compounding, longer-horizon investments like organic/content and brand), and to rebalance the tier split as the business matures.

## Professional Guidance
- Do not evaluate every channel on the same time horizon; a channel mix review should segment channels by their natural payback cycle before comparing returns.
- Treat channel diversification as a risk-management discipline, not just an efficiency question — concentration in a single channel exposes the business to platform/auction/partner risk.
- Reassess channel mix when the buyer profile changes (e.g., moving upmarket typically shifts optimal mix toward events/ABM/partner and away from broad paid/social).
- Use complementary-effect reasoning: cutting organic/content budget too aggressively can quietly raise paid channel CAC over subsequent quarters.

## Examples
**Fast-payback tier example**: 35% of budget to paid search/social because it reliably fills near-term pipeline gaps with sub-3-month payback.

**Compounding tier example**: 20% of budget sustained in organic/content despite a longer payback, because trailing 18-month data shows organic-sourced CAC is 60% lower than paid.

**Concentration risk example**: A plan showing 70% of budget in a single partner channel is flagged for diversification risk even though that channel currently has the best CAC, because a partner relationship change could remove most of the pipeline overnight.

**Upmarket shift example**: As average deal size grows from $5,000 to $50,000 ACV, the recommended mix shifts from broad paid/social toward ABM, events, and partner channels that suit a smaller number of higher-value target accounts.

## Common Mistakes
- Comparing a fast-payback channel (paid search) against a slow-payback channel (events, organic) using the same short measurement window.
- Cutting brand/organic investment entirely during a budget squeeze, which often raises paid CAC in following quarters.
- Concentrating budget in a single high-performing channel without accounting for platform, auction, or partner concentration risk.
- Applying an ABM strategy to a market with a large number of low-value buyers, where broad-reach channels are structurally more efficient.
- Ignoring how the buyer's average deal size and account concentration should drive channel mix, defaulting instead to "what we did last year."

## Best Practices
- Segment the channel portfolio into fast/medium/slow payback tiers and evaluate each tier on its own appropriate time horizon.
- Deliberately protect a compounding-asset allocation (organic/content/brand) even when short-term pressure favors paid channels.
- Cap any single channel's share of total budget (commonly in the 40-60% range) absent a strong, explicit justification, to manage concentration risk.
- Revisit channel mix whenever the target buyer profile, average deal size, or competitive landscape shifts materially.

## Summary
Channel mix strategy is about matching each channel type's structural characteristics — speed, scalability, compounding value, and concentration risk — to the business's buyer profile and planning horizon. A resilient budget allocation balances fast-payback channels against compounding, longer-horizon investments, and avoids over-concentration in any single channel regardless of its current-period performance.
