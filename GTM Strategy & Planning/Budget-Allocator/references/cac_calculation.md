# CAC Calculation

## Purpose
This document provides standalone reference knowledge on how to calculate, segment, and interpret Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC), including fully-loaded CAC, CAC by channel, and CAC payback period, so that budget allocation decisions rest on a consistent, defensible cost basis.

## Introduction
Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) is the average cost required to acquire one new paying customer over a given period. It is the single most important input to a GTM budget model because it directly determines whether spend on a channel is efficient, and it is the metric most often calculated inconsistently — different teams count different costs, making cross-channel and cross-period comparisons unreliable unless the calculation basis is standardized.

## Detailed Explanation

### Basic CAC Formula
CAC = Total Acquisition Costs (period) / Number of New Customers Acquired (same period)

### Fully-Loaded CAC
Fully-loaded CAC includes every cost that contributes to acquiring the customer, not just working media/ad spend:
- Paid media / ad spend
- Content production and creative costs
- Marketing and sales tooling/software costs (CRM, ad platforms, analytics) allocated proportionally
- Salaries and commissions for marketing and sales staff involved in acquisition (fully or partially allocated based on time spent on new-business acquisition vs. retention/expansion)
- Agency and contractor fees
- Events, sponsorships, travel tied to acquisition activity
- Partner/referral fees or revenue share tied to new-customer acquisition

Fully-loaded CAC is almost always meaningfully higher than "media-only" CAC — often by 1.5-3x — because salaries and tooling are frequently omitted in quick estimates. Always state which cost components are included when reporting a CAC figure.

### CAC by Channel
CAC should be calculated per channel, not only as a blended average, because a blended CAC hides which channels are efficient and which are not:
CAC(channel) = Total Acquisition Costs Attributable to Channel / New Customers Attributed to Channel

Attribution challenges are real: a customer may touch paid search, then a webinar, then a sales call before converting. Common attribution approaches, from simplest to most rigorous:
- **Last-touch**: 100% of credit to the final channel before conversion. Simple but overweights bottom-of-funnel channels.
- **First-touch**: 100% of credit to the channel that first introduced the prospect. Overweights top-of-funnel/awareness channels.
- **Linear/multi-touch**: credit split evenly (or weighted) across all touchpoints in the journey. More accurate but requires tracking infrastructure.
- **Channel-level cohort approach**: when touch-level tracking isn't available, approximate by allocating cost and customers to the channel that generated the initial qualified lead, and note the limitation.

### CAC Payback Period
CAC Payback Period = CAC / (Average Monthly Revenue per Customer x Gross Margin %)

This measures how many months it takes for the gross margin generated by a new customer to repay the cost of acquiring them. A shorter payback period means the business recovers acquisition cost — and can reinvest it — faster.
- Payback under ~12 months is generally considered healthy for many subscription/recurring-revenue models; capital-intensive or long-sales-cycle businesses may tolerate longer paybacks if LTV is high.
- Payback period should always be quoted alongside the margin assumption used, since it is highly sensitive to gross margin.

## Professional Guidance
- Always specify the time window used (e.g., trailing 3 months, trailing 12 months) — CAC calculated over different windows is not comparable.
- When comparing CAC across channels, use the same attribution method for every channel in the comparison; mixing last-touch for one channel and multi-touch for another invalidates the comparison.
- Segment CAC by customer segment (e.g., SMB vs. enterprise) when the underlying sales motion differs materially, since a single blended CAC can mask very different unit economics.
- Track CAC trend (quarter-over-quarter direction), not just the current-period value; a rising CAC trend is an early warning sign even if the absolute number is still "acceptable."

## Examples
**Fully-loaded CAC example**: A channel spent $80,000 on media, $15,000 on creative, and had $25,000 of allocated sales/marketing salary time in a quarter, acquiring 40 customers. Fully-loaded CAC = ($80,000 + $15,000 + $25,000) / 40 = $3,000 per customer, versus a media-only CAC of $2,000 — a 50% understatement if salaries and creative were excluded.

**CAC by channel example**: Paid search CAC = $2,200; organic/content CAC = $900; events/field CAC = $5,400; partner-referral CAC = $1,600. Even though events CAC is highest, if events also drive the largest average deal size, payback and LTV:CAC must be checked before concluding events is "worse."

**Payback period example**: CAC = $3,000; average monthly revenue per customer = $500; gross margin = 70%. Monthly gross margin dollars = $350. Payback period = $3,000 / $350 ≈ 8.6 months.

## Common Mistakes
- Reporting media-only CAC and calling it "CAC" without disclosing that salaries, tools, and overhead are excluded.
- Comparing CAC across channels using inconsistent attribution methods.
- Ignoring CAC trend over time and treating a single-period snapshot as the full picture.
- Failing to segment CAC by customer type when sales motions differ significantly (self-serve vs. enterprise field sales).
- Computing payback period without stating the gross margin assumption used.

## Best Practices
- Standardize on one attribution method across the organization and document it explicitly in every CAC report.
- Report CAC alongside payback period and LTV:CAC together — a single metric in isolation can mislead.
- Refresh CAC calculations at least monthly for fast-moving channels (paid media) and at least quarterly for slower-moving ones (partner, events).
- When historical data for a new channel doesn't exist, use category benchmark ranges and label them explicitly as estimates pending real data.

## Summary
CAC is the foundation of any GTM budget model, but only when calculated consistently. Fully-loaded CAC captures the true cost of acquisition; channel-level CAC reveals where spend is efficient; and payback period translates CAC into a cash-flow-relevant metric. Every CAC figure used in a budget allocation exercise should disclose its cost components, attribution method, and time window.
