# OKR Framework: Objectives, Key Results, Grading, and Cascading

> **Purpose:** Explain how to draft objectives and key results, grade them objectively, and cascade them across teams so the quarterly roadmap has measurable success criteria.

## Introduction

Objectives and Key Results (OKRs) translate a roadmap's themes into a small number of qualitative goals (Objectives) paired with measurable outcomes (Key Results). OKRs are the success-metrics layer of the quarterly roadmap: while the roadmap and milestone plan describe what work will happen and when, OKRs describe what result that work is expected to produce and how success will be measured and graded.

## Detailed Explanation

### Objectives

An Objective is a short, qualitative, ambitious statement of what the team wants to achieve in the quarter. Good objectives are:
- **Inspirational but bounded to the quarter** — not a permanent mission statement, not a task.
- **Owned by a team or function**, even if it supports a larger company-wide theme.
- **Non-numeric** — the numbers belong in the key results, not the objective itself.

Example pattern: "Establish credibility in [NEW SEGMENT]" or "Make onboarding the fastest path to first value."

### Key Results

Key Results are the measurable outcomes that indicate the objective has been achieved. Each objective typically has 2–4 key results. Good key results:
- **Are numeric and independently verifiable** (a percentage, a count, a rate, a binary milestone completion where genuinely binary).
- **Describe an outcome, not an activity.** "Launch three campaigns" is an activity; "Increase qualified leads from campaign channels by [X%]" is an outcome.
- **Are gradable on a consistent scale**, commonly 0.0–1.0 (or 0–100%), at quarter close.

### Grading

At quarter close-out, each key result is graded against its target, typically using a 0.0–1.0 scale where 1.0 is full achievement. A common calibration convention (borrowed from widely used OKR practice) treats a 0.6–0.7 average across key results as a healthy, appropriately ambitious outcome — consistently scoring 1.0 across the board over multiple quarters can indicate the targets were not ambitious enough, while consistently scoring near 0 indicates targets were unrealistic or execution broke down. Grading should be evidence-based: cite the actual metric value achieved against the target, not a subjective impression.

### Cascading

Cascading means connecting team-level or function-level OKRs to the broader roadmap themes without simply copying a single top-level OKR down to every team unchanged. Effective cascading:
- Starts from the roadmap's themes (see `roadmap_framework.md`) and asks each contributing function what its objective and key results are in service of that theme.
- Allows a single theme to be supported by different objectives per function (e.g., marketing's objective for a "new segment" theme might focus on qualified pipeline generation, while product's objective focuses on feature readiness for that segment).
- Avoids forcing every function into an identical OKR structure when their actual contribution differs.
- Keeps the total number of objectives per team small (typically 2–4) to preserve focus; a long list of objectives is not an OKR set, it is a task list in disguise.

### OKRs vs. Milestones

OKRs and milestones serve different purposes and should not be conflated. A milestone is a dated checkpoint confirming a deliverable was completed. A key result is a measure of outcome achieved, which may or may not correlate directly with milestone completion (a milestone can be completed on time and the associated key result still miss its target, which is itself useful information).

## Professional Guidance

- Draft key results before finalizing objective wording — if you cannot state a measurable key result, the objective may be too vague or actually a task.
- Cap objectives at 2–4 per team per quarter; more indicates insufficient prioritization.
- Always specify the measurement method alongside the numeric target (e.g., "measured via [DATA SOURCE/DASHBOARD]") so grading is not disputed at quarter close.
- When cascading, check that the sum of function-level key results plausibly supports the parent theme rather than being an arbitrary restatement.
- Treat OKRs as a planning and alignment tool, not a performance-review weapon; grading below 1.0 on an appropriately ambitious key result is expected and healthy.

## Examples

*Illustrative and generic — no real company implied.*

- Objective: "Establish credibility in [NEW SEGMENT]." Key Results: "Generate [X] qualified opportunities from [NEW SEGMENT] (measured via CRM pipeline report)"; "Achieve a [Y%] positive response rate on segment-specific outreach"; "Publish [Z] segment-specific proof points (case studies, references) validated by sales."
- Objective: "Make onboarding the fastest path to first value." Key Results: "Reduce median time-to-first-value from [current] to [target] days"; "Increase onboarding completion rate from [current%] to [target%]"; "Reduce onboarding-related support tickets by [X%]."
- Cascading example: the roadmap theme "Expand partner-sourced pipeline" cascades to a marketing objective focused on partner enablement content completion and a sales objective focused on partner-sourced qualified pipeline volume — related but distinct key results supporting one theme.

## Common Mistakes

- Writing objectives with numbers baked in, then treating the objective itself as the key result (blurs accountability for the actual measurable outcome).
- Setting more than 4-5 objectives per team, diluting focus into a disguised task list.
- Choosing key results that measure activity ("run X campaigns") instead of outcome ("increase Y by Z%").
- Cascading by copying the same OKR verbatim to every team regardless of their actual contribution.
- Skipping the grading step at quarter close, so learnings are never captured for the next planning cycle.

## Best Practices

- Keep objectives qualitative and key results quantitative.
- Limit each team to 2–4 objectives and each objective to 2–4 key results.
- Specify the measurement method for every key result at drafting time, not at grading time.
- Cascade from roadmap themes to function-specific objectives rather than forcing identical OKRs everywhere.
- Grade with evidence at quarter close and feed the result into the next quarter's planning inputs.

## Summary

OKRs supply the measurable success layer of a quarterly roadmap: objectives capture qualitative intent, key results make that intent measurable and gradable, and cascading connects team-level OKRs to the roadmap's strategic themes without forcing uniformity. Objective grading at quarter close closes the loop, turning each quarter's plan into evidence that improves the next one.
