# Quarterly Planning Cadence and Cross-Functional Alignment

> **Purpose:** Explain the methodology and cadence for running quarterly planning cycles, and how to align marketing, sales, product, and customer success around a single execution plan.

## Introduction

Quarterly planning is the operating rhythm that translates strategy into execution. A quarter (roughly 13 weeks) is long enough to deliver meaningful outcomes and short enough to react to market feedback. This reference describes the cadence of quarterly planning — from kickoff through mid-quarter review to close-out — and the cross-functional alignment practices that keep marketing, sales, product, and customer success moving in the same direction. It is the operating methodology that underlies every roadmap this skill produces.

## Detailed Explanation

### The Planning Cadence

A healthy quarterly cadence typically has four phases:

1. **Pre-quarter planning (1–2 weeks before quarter start).** Strategy inputs are gathered, initiatives are inventoried, prioritization is run, and draft roadmaps, milestones, and OKRs are produced. Cross-functional stakeholders review and commit to the plan before the quarter begins.
2. **Quarter kickoff (week 1).** The finalized roadmap, owner assignments, and OKRs are communicated to all contributing teams. Dependencies are confirmed with upstream and downstream owners.
3. **Mid-quarter review (around week 6–7).** Progress against milestones and key results is assessed. Risks that have materialized are addressed. Re-prioritization happens here if capacity, market conditions, or dependencies have shifted — this is a planned checkpoint, not an emergency measure.
4. **Quarter close-out (final 1–2 weeks).** Key results are graded, milestones are marked complete or carried forward, and lessons learned are captured to inform the next quarter's planning inputs.

### Cross-Functional Alignment

A quarterly roadmap that only reflects one function's view (e.g., only product, or only marketing) will fail at the seams. Alignment requires:

- **A shared initiative inventory.** Every team's proposed work for the quarter is listed in one place before prioritization begins, not siloed in separate documents.
- **Named upstream/downstream relationships.** For every initiative that depends on another team's output (e.g., sales enablement content depends on product feature completion), the dependency is explicit, with an owner on each side and a required-by date.
- **A single source of truth for dates.** Marketing campaign dates, sales enablement readiness, and product release dates must reconcile to one timeline, not multiple conflicting calendars.
- **Regular synchronization points.** Weekly or bi-weekly cross-functional check-ins during the quarter catch drift early, before it becomes a missed milestone.
- **Escalation paths for conflicts.** When two teams have competing priorities for shared capacity (e.g., a shared design or data resource), there must be a clear decision-maker and a fast path to resolution.

### Time-Boxing Discipline

Time-boxing means every initiative gets a bounded window, not an open-ended commitment. This forces trade-off decisions up front (see `prioritization_frameworks.md`) rather than letting scope creep absorb all available time. A time-boxed quarter should reserve some buffer (commonly 10–20% of capacity) for unplanned work, support escalations, or newly discovered risks.

## Professional Guidance

- Treat the pre-quarter planning phase as non-negotiable — a roadmap built without cross-functional input during planning will surface conflicts mid-quarter instead of before it starts.
- Calendar the mid-quarter review before the quarter begins; do not leave it to be scheduled reactively.
- Keep the initiative inventory and the roadmap in the same document lineage so priorities can be traced back to their original request.
- When teams operate on different cadences (e.g., product on two-week sprints, marketing on campaign cycles), anchor all of them to the same quarterly milestone dates for planning purposes.
- Reserve explicit buffer capacity; a 100%-allocated quarter has no room to absorb the first risk that materializes.

## Examples

*Illustrative and generic — no real company implied.*

- A cross-functional team planning a new market entry sets a pre-quarter planning window two weeks before quarter start, during which marketing, sales, and product each submit their initiative list into one shared inventory before prioritization.
- A mid-quarter review reveals that a product dependency for a sales enablement milestone has slipped two weeks; the team re-sequences the affected downstream milestones during the scheduled review rather than waiting until quarter close-out.
- A team reserves 15% of quarterly capacity as buffer; when a customer-escalation-driven initiative arises mid-quarter, it is absorbed using the buffer instead of displacing a committed milestone.

## Common Mistakes

- Running planning inside a single function and distributing the finished plan to other teams for information only, rather than input.
- Treating the mid-quarter review as optional or skipping it when things "seem fine."
- Allocating 100% of capacity with no buffer, guaranteeing that the first unplanned event causes a missed milestone.
- Letting each function keep its own calendar of dates instead of reconciling to one timeline.
- Failing to define an escalation path, so cross-team conflicts stall silently instead of being resolved quickly.

## Best Practices

- Anchor all functions to one quarterly calendar with shared milestone dates.
- Build the initiative inventory jointly, before prioritization, not after.
- Schedule the mid-quarter review as a fixed checkpoint at plan creation time.
- Reserve buffer capacity explicitly rather than assuming slack will appear.
- Document every cross-functional dependency with named upstream/downstream owners and a required-by date.
- Capture close-out lessons learned as an input to the next quarter's planning cycle.

## Summary

Quarterly planning is a repeatable four-phase cadence — pre-quarter planning, kickoff, mid-quarter review, and close-out — built on cross-functional alignment rather than single-function scheduling. The discipline of a shared initiative inventory, explicit dependency ownership, a single timeline, and reserved buffer capacity is what allows a quarterly roadmap to survive contact with reality. This cadence is the backbone that the roadmap structuring, milestone planning, OKR, prioritization, and risk management practices described in the other reference files all operate within.
