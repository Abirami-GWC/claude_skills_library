# CTA Types by Mechanic

## Comment-driving CTAs
What works: a specific, low-effort, opinionated question tied directly to the post's content. People comment when the question is easy to answer in one line and touches something they have a real opinion on.
- "What's your tier count right now?" (specific number, easy to answer)
- "Which of these have you actually tried?"
- "Disagree? Tell me why."
Avoid: open-ended abstract questions ("What do you think about marketing?") — too much effort, no comments.

## Share-driving CTAs
What works: content the reader wants their own audience to see — usually because it reflects well on them to share it, or it's useful to someone specific they're thinking of.
- "Send this to someone who's about to make this mistake."
- "If your team is still doing this, they need to see it."
Avoid: "please share this post" — asking directly rarely works; give a *reason* to share instead.

## Save/bookmark-driving CTAs
What works: framing the post as a reference to come back to — usually paired with list/framework/how-to content.
- "Save this for the next time you're stuck on [specific problem]."
- "Bookmark this — you'll want the checklist when you're actually doing it."
Avoid: vague "save for later!" with no reason given.

## Follow-driving CTAs
What works: a promise of a specific, recurring value the reader wants more of — not "follow for more content."
- "I break down one pricing mistake like this every week — follow if that's useful."
- "More of this kind of teardown coming — follow to catch the next one."
Avoid: generic "follow for more!" with no specificity about what "more" is.

## Click-driving CTAs
What works: for platforms/goals where a link is the ask — be direct about what's on the other side and why it's worth the click.
- "Full breakdown (with the actual numbers) is linked in the first comment."
- "Wrote the full teardown here: [link]" (link in comment for LinkedIn/Facebook to protect reach)
Avoid: "Link in bio!" with zero context on what's there.

## Mixing types
Pick exactly one CTA type per post. Trying to drive comments and shares and clicks in the same closing line dilutes all three.
