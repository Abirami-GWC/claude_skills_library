# Deliberate practice and stakeholder qualification

Background on why `handlers/objection-roleplay-simulator.md` and
`playbooks/negotiation-endgame-playbook.md` are structured the way they are.

## Why the simulated prospect can't be a pushover or a caricature

Deliberate practice only builds skill if the resistance is realistic. A prospect that folds
immediately teaches a rep nothing about handling real pushback; a prospect that's needlessly
combative teaches bad habits (overly defensive responses that would read as aggressive on a real
call). The handler's instruction to raise a real concern, listen, then partially concede or raise
a natural follow-up is what keeps the practice transferable to an actual call.

## Why coaching notes are separated from the in-character reaction

Mixing in-character response and coaching feedback in the same line makes it hard for a rep to
tell what's "the prospect talking" versus "the coach talking," which weakens the practice value of
both. Keeping them visually distinct lets the rep experience the pushback as realistic in the
moment, then step back and evaluate it separately.

## When objections cluster, look for a missing stakeholder

A pattern worth recognizing in `playbooks/negotiation-endgame-playbook.md`: when a prospect raises
several small, inconsistent objections in the same call rather than one clear one, it's often a
sign that the person on the call isn't the actual decision-maker and is relaying secondhand
concerns imperfectly. This is a MEDDIC-adjacent qualification gap (missing Economic Buyer or
Champion), not a persuasion problem — no amount of a better talk track fixes a deal that's stuck
because the wrong person is being convinced. The fix is identifying and involving that missing
stakeholder directly, not answering each small objection in isolation.

## Repeated slipping signature dates are a qualification signal, not a scheduling one

If a signature date keeps slipping by a few days repeatedly, it's usually a sign the champion
doesn't have the internal authority they implied earlier, rather than a simple scheduling
conflict. Practicing this pattern in roleplay should train a rep to ask "who else needs to be
looped in" rather than keep pushing the same contact for a new date.
