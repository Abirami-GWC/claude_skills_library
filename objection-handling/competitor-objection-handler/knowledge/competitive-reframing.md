# Competitive reframing

Background on the methodology behind `handlers/competitor-objection-handler.md`'s response
structure — useful when a user asks why the handler is built this way, or wants to draft a new
battlecard entry that fits the same logic.

## Challenger-style reframing, not feature-matching

Rather than matching the prospect's frame (e.g. agreeing to compare feature-for-feature against a
competitor), the response frame in this handler shifts the axis of comparison — unified
management, total cost, operational overhead — to one where the differentiation is strongest.
This only works when the reframe is genuine and defensible; forcing a reframe onto an objection
where it doesn't fit reads as evasive, which is why the handler's hard rule requires every claim
to be verified before it goes in the battlecard table.

## Concede first, then differentiate

Every battlecard row is structured as: what they do well → how we differentiate → a question to
ask back. Conceding the competitor's genuine strength first isn't just politeness — it signals
credibility. A prospect who's done their own research will notice immediately if a rep disputes a
well-known competitor strength, and that costs more trust than it's worth. The differentiation
angle lands better once the concession is out of the way.

## Why end on a question, not a claim

Ending the response with a question (e.g. "how much time does your team spend managing policies
across appliances today?") does two things: it re-centers the conversation on the prospect's
actual situation instead of a head-to-head debate, and it surfaces information that shapes how the
rest of the differentiation pitch should go. A comparison that ends on an assertion invites a
counter-assertion; one that ends on a question invites information.
