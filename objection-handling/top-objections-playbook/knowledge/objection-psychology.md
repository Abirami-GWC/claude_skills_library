# Objection psychology

Background on *why* general objections happen, underlying the response frames in
`handlers/top-objections-playbook.md`. Read this when an objection needs a response beyond the
table entry, or when asked to explain the reasoning rather than just produce a response.

## Objections are rarely the real concern

A stated objection is usually a proxy for something the prospect either hasn't fully identified or
doesn't want to say outright. Two common gaps between what's said and what's meant:

- **"We need to think about it"** almost never means literal deliberation time — it means there's
  one specific unresolved concern the prospect hasn't named. This is why row 3 in
  `handlers/top-objections-playbook.md` asks a narrowing question instead of just backing off.
- **"We're happy with what we have"** is a status-quo bias statement, not necessarily a true
  absence of pain — most teams that later switch vendors said something like this earlier in the
  relationship.

## Why "acknowledge, then ask" beats "acknowledge, then argue"

Every row in `handlers/top-objections-playbook.md` follows a shape: acknowledge the objection as
reasonable, then ask a question rather than immediately arguing a counter-point. This isn't just
tone-softening — it serves two functional purposes:

1. **It surfaces the real objection** behind the stated one before committing to a specific
   counter-argument, which avoids wasting the one rebuttal on the wrong target.
2. **It keeps the prospect talking**, which surfaces information (budget authority, timeline,
   internal politics) that a purely persuasive response would never elicit.

This is also the SPIN-style questioning pattern (Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-payoff):
instead of responding to an objection with a claim, the question moves the prospect from stating a
surface problem toward articulating its cost — that's what makes "what's the one thing you're
unsure about" more effective than a reassurance.

## Loss aversion and the "no fire yet" trap

Prospects tend to weigh the risk of switching (a known, near-term cost) more heavily than the risk
of staying (a diffuse, longer-term cost). This is why row 7 ("we're happy with what we have") asks
about incidents or near-misses rather than pitching features — the goal is surfacing a concrete,
already-experienced cost of the status quo, which counteracts loss aversion better than a
hypothetical future benefit does.

## Caution: don't over-diagnose

This file explains common patterns, not universal rules. A stated objection can also just be the
literal, true concern — not every "we don't have budget" is secretly something else. Use this
knowledge to ask a good clarifying question, not to assume you already know what the prospect
"really" means before they've said it.
