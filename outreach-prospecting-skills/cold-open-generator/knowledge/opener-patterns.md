# Opener Pattern Catalog

## 1. The Direct Observation
State a specific, verifiable fact about the prospect/company with no commentary
yet. Let the specificity do the work.
> "Noticed Acme's job posting for a 'Data Reliability Engineer' went up last week."

**Best for:** hiring signals, product launches, structural changes.

## 2. The Reaction/Response
Reference something the prospect said/wrote and respond to the substance of it,
not just that it exists.
> "Your point in [talk/post] about [specific idea] tracks with what we're seeing
> at [related] companies too."

**Best for:** content signals (articles, talks, podcasts).
**Guardrail:** the response must engage with the actual content, not just say
"loved your post!"

## 3. The Sharp Question
Ask a specific, answerable-in-one-line question that only makes sense given real
knowledge of their situation.
> "Curious how the team's handling schema drift now that you're shipping two new
> data sources a month?"

**Best for:** cold + low-context situations where a strong observation isn't available.

## 4. The Peer Reference
Reference a comparable company/persona's situation, tied to something specific
about the prospect that makes the comparison relevant. Only use with real,
confirmed examples.
> "A few [role] at similar-stage [industry] companies have mentioned the same
> [specific problem] once they cross [specific threshold] — seems like Acme might
> be right at that point."

**Best for:** later touches or when strong peer data exists.

## 5. The Structural Insight
Point out something inferred from public, structural information (org chart
changes, tooling choices, expansion) rather than a single event.
> "Saw Acme's engineering team roughly doubled this year based on LinkedIn headcount."

**Best for:** growth-stage companies, structural/timing-based relevance.

## Formulas to avoid entirely
- Generic flattery with no substance ("Loved what you're building!")
- Weather/small talk openers ("Hope you're having a great week!")
- Sender-centric openers ("My name is X and I work at Y, we help companies like yours...")
- Overly broad industry statements with no company-specific tie ("Companies in
  SaaS are facing more data challenges than ever.")
