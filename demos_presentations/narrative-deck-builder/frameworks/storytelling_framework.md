# Storytelling Framework

This is the most important file in this skill — it's the narrative backbone
every deck must follow, regardless of how the input arrives or what order the
customer mentioned things in.

## Story Flow (do not skip or reorder these beats)

1. **Introduce the customer's business/industry context.** Open with the
   world the customer lives in, not your product. Frame the stakes at an
   industry level before getting specific ("Modern Security Challenges in
   Retail" — not "About WatchGuard").

2. **Explain their current challenges.** Get specific to this customer's
   stated pains, in their own language where possible.

3. **Show why the existing solution is insufficient.** This is the "tension"
   beat of the story — the gap between what they have and what they need.
   Frame this factually and respectfully; never disparage the incumbent
   vendor by name-calling, just show the gap relative to the stated pains.

4. **Introduce the recommended solution.** This is the turning point — pivot
   from problem to solution. Map each stated pain to how the solution
   addresses it specifically, not a generic capability list.

5. **Demonstrate business value.** Translate the technical solution into
   business terms: reduced downtime, reduced risk, reduced operational cost,
   compliance achieved. This should connect back to whatever business goal was
   stated in discovery, if known.

6. **Support with a customer success story.** Proof that this isn't
   theoretical — a real (or clearly placeholder) example of the story already
   playing out successfully for a similar customer.

7. **Explain implementation.** Address the "how hard is this to actually do"
   question — migration path, typical timeline, what the customer can expect.
   Omit this beat if there's no current-vendor migration context.

8. **Finish with next steps.** A concrete, low-friction call to action —
   never leave the story hanging without a clear "here's what happens next."

## Why this order matters

Skipping straight to "introduce the solution" (beat 4) without beats 1-3 turns
the deck back into a feature brochure — the customer hasn't been given a
reason to care yet. Skipping the success story (beat 6) leaves the pivot
(beat 4-5) feeling unproven. Skipping next steps (beat 8) leaves no clear path
to a follow-up meeting or proposal.

## Adapting to audience

- **Business/executive audience:** spend more narrative weight on beats 1, 2,
  5, and 6 (business stakes and business value). Keep beat 4 and 7 (solution
  detail, implementation) high-level.
- **Technical audience:** beats 1-3 can be shorter, but don't skip them
  entirely — even technical buyers respond better to a problem-first framing.
  Spend more weight on beat 4 (solution) and 7 (implementation detail).

## Common failure mode to avoid

A slide titled after a product or feature name in isolation ("Firebox
Features", "ThreatSync Overview", "Pricing") is a signal the story has broken
down into a brochure. Every slide title should describe a story beat or a
customer-facing outcome, never a product module.
